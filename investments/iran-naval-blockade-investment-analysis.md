# Iran Naval Blockade: Macro-Economic & Investment Analysis

**Date:** April 2026
**Context:** US naval blockade on Iranian oil exports (~1.5-2M bbl/day removed from global supply)

---

## 1. Oil Price Impact

### Supply math

- Global oil demand: ~103M bbl/day (2025-2026 estimates)
- Iran exports pre-blockade: ~1.5-2.0M bbl/day (primarily to China, India, Turkey)
- Supply deficit as % of global: **1.5-2.0%**
- OPEC+ spare capacity: ~3-5M bbl/day (mostly Saudi Arabia and UAE), but political willingness to deploy is uncertain
- Net effective tightening after partial OPEC+ response: likely **0.5-1.0M bbl/day**

A 1-2% supply shock historically produces **15-40% price spikes** in the short term.

### Historical precedent

| Episode | Supply removed | Price move | Duration |
|---------|---------------|------------|----------|
| **2012 EU/US sanctions** | ~1.0M bbl/day (Iran dropped from 2.5M to 1.5M) | Brent peaked at $125 (from ~$95) = **+32%** | 18-24 months elevated |
| **2018 Trump JCPOA withdrawal** | ~1.5M bbl/day (Iran dropped from 2.5M to ~0.5M) | Brent hit $86 (from ~$65) = **+32%**, then fell when waivers granted | 6-12 months |
| **1979 Iranian Revolution** | ~2.5M bbl/day | Price doubled from $14 to $31 = **+120%** | Multi-year |
| **2026 blockade (current)** | ~1.5-2.0M bbl/day | Brent spiked to **$119.50** intraday (Mar 2026), moderated to ~$100 | Ongoing |

### Price target estimates

- **Base case (blockade sustained 6-12 months):** Brent $90-110, WTI $85-105
- **Escalation case (Strait of Hormuz disrupted):** Brent $130-150+, WTI $125-145
- **De-escalation case (blockade lifted within 3 months):** Brent falls back to $70-80

### WTI vs Brent spread

- Brent typically trades at a **$3-8 premium** to WTI
- In Iran-related disruptions, the Brent-WTI spread **widens** because:
  - Brent is the international benchmark; Iranian supply disruption affects seaborne crude directly
  - WTI benefits from abundant US domestic supply
  - Historical: In 2012 sanctions, spread widened to **$15-25**
- **Trade implication:** Long Brent / short WTI spread trade profits from Iran escalation

## 2. US Dollar Implications

### Petrodollar dynamics

The petrodollar system means oil is priced and settled in USD globally. When Iranian oil is replaced by Saudi, US, or other producers, the key effects are:

1. **More oil flows through USD-denominated channels** (Iran was selling to China partly in yuan/barter)
2. **Higher oil prices increase global USD demand** — every importing country needs more dollars
3. **US producers benefit from higher prices** — increased dollar earnings, improved trade balance for energy sector

### Historical USD/oil price correlation

The relationship is complex and **not linear**:

- **1979-1985:** Oil spike → USD strengthened dramatically (DXY rose ~50%)
- **2008:** Oil spike → USD initially weakened, then surged during flight to safety
- **2014-2016:** Oil collapsed → USD strengthened (counter-intuitive: driven by shale making US energy-independent)
- **2018 Iran sanctions:** USD strengthened modestly (DXY rose ~7% that year)

**Key insight:** The USD tends to strengthen when:
- Oil disruptions are *outside* the US (US is now a net exporter)
- Risk aversion drives safe-haven demand
- Higher oil prices hurt oil-importing economies (EUR, JPY, INR weaken)

### DXY implications in this scenario

**Bullish USD factors:**
- US is world's largest oil producer — higher prices improve US terms of trade
- Safe-haven flows during geopolitical uncertainty
- Oil-importing countries (EU, Japan, India) face worsening trade balances
- If Fed pauses/delays rate cuts due to inflation, rate differential supports USD

**Expected DXY move:** +3-8% over 6-12 months if blockade persists

## 3. Winners and Losers by Country

### Winners

**Saudi Arabia & UAE**
- Capture market share from Iran's lost barrels
- Higher prices on existing production = massive revenue windfall
- Saudi breakeven budget price: ~$80/bbl — prices above this are pure surplus
- Risk: pressure from US to increase output, dampening prices

**United States**
- World's largest producer (~13M bbl/day)
- Shale producers profitable at $50-70; $90-100 = enormous free cash flow
- Energy sector jobs, tax revenue, royalties all increase
- Strategic advantage: energy independence insulates US economy
- Risk: higher domestic gasoline prices are politically costly

**Russia**
- Sells more crude to China/India to fill Iran gap (already doing so)
- Higher global prices benefit Russian revenues despite Western sanctions
- But: Russia's oil trades at $10-15 discount (Urals vs Brent)

**Norway, Canada, Brazil**
- Major producers outside OPEC
- Direct beneficiaries of higher prices
- Norway's sovereign wealth fund ($1.7T+) grows faster
- Canadian oil sands become more profitable
- Brazil's pre-salt offshore production gains margin

### Losers

**China**
- Was buying ~1.0-1.5M bbl/day of discounted Iranian crude (some at $10-15/bbl below Brent)
- Must now source from Saudi, Russia, US at **higher prices**
- China's annual oil import bill: ~$300B+ at current prices; a $20/bbl increase adds **~$70B/year**
- Likely response:
  - Increase Russian crude purchases (but Russia has capacity limits)
  - Negotiate long-term Saudi contracts
  - Release strategic petroleum reserves
  - Accelerate EV and renewable transition
  - Possible diplomatic retaliation or sanctions evasion

**India**
- 2nd largest buyer of Iranian oil after China
- Was importing ~0.3-0.5M bbl/day from Iran at discounted prices
- India imports ~85% of its crude needs — massive cost sensitivity
- Impact: wider current account deficit, INR depreciation, imported inflation
- Response: diversify to Iraq, Saudi, UAE, Russia; pressure for US crude imports

**Europe (EU)**
- Already weaned off Iranian crude post-2012
- But tighter global supply = higher prices for all imports
- Natural gas prices co-move with oil — double exposure
- ECB faces inflation pressure vs. weak growth = **stagflation risk**
- Germany, Italy most vulnerable (manufacturing + import dependence)

**Japan & South Korea**
- Near-100% import dependent for energy
- LNG prices correlated with oil prices (~6-month lag)
- Both had small Iranian crude imports; impact is indirect via price levels
- JPY and KRW likely weaken against USD
- Central banks face imported inflation

**Turkey**
- Was buying ~0.1-0.2M bbl/day from Iran (often at discount)
- Already high inflation; higher energy costs exacerbate
- TRY under pressure; current account deficit widens
- Geographically exposed to Middle East instability

## 4. Inflation Implications

### Transmission mechanism

```
Higher oil → higher gasoline/diesel → higher transport costs →
  → higher food prices (transport, fertilizer)
  → higher manufacturing input costs
  → broader CPI increase
  → wage pressure → second-round effects
```

### Historical inflation impact

- **2008:** Oil at $147 → US CPI peaked at 5.6%
- **2011-2012:** Oil at $100-125 → US CPI at 3.0-3.9%
- **2022:** Post-Russia/Ukraine → US CPI hit 9.1%

### Current scenario estimate

- Oil at $90-110 sustained → adds **0.5-1.5 percentage points** to headline CPI
- Core inflation impact smaller (~0.3-0.5pp) but non-trivial
- If Strait of Hormuz disrupted and oil hits $130+, CPI could add **2-3pp**

### Fed response scenarios

| Oil price | CPI impact | Fed response |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| $85-95 | Modest (+0.5pp) | Proceed with planned rate cuts, slowly |
| $95-110 | Moderate (+1.0pp) | **Pause rate cuts**; hawkish rhetoric |
| $110-130 | Significant (+1.5pp) | **No cuts in 2026**; possible rate hike discussion |
| $130+ | Severe (+2-3pp) | **Rate hikes back on table**; stagflation risk |

The current trajectory (~$100/bbl) suggests the Fed **pauses rate cuts** and maintains a higher-for-longer stance.

## 6. Risk Factors

### Risk 1: Blockade is temporary/lifted

- **Probability:** 25-35%
- **Trigger:** Iran-US negotiations succeed (talks have been ongoing per Wikipedia sources; Iran signaled willingness to negotiate as early as March 2025)
- **Market impact:** Oil drops $15-25 rapidly; energy stocks give back 10-20%; USD reverses
- **Hedge:** Use options with defined risk rather than outright futures positions; trail stops on energy longs

### Risk 2: OPEC+ increases production to compensate

- **Probability:** 40-50%
- **Trigger:** Saudi Arabia has ~2-3M bbl/day spare capacity; UAE has ~1M
- **Market impact:** Could offset 50-100% of Iranian supply loss; oil falls back to $75-85
- **Reality check:** Saudi historically prefers higher prices; they'll increase *slowly* to maximize revenue
- **Key indicator:** Watch OPEC+ monthly meeting decisions and Saudi OSP (Official Selling Price)

### Risk 3: Global recession reduces demand

- **Probability:** 20-30%
- **Trigger:** Higher oil prices themselves cause demand destruction; China slowdown accelerates; trade war intensifies
- **Market impact:** Oil could drop to $60-70 despite supply constraints (demand destruction > supply loss)
- **Historical parallel:** 2008 — oil hit $147, then crashed to $34 within 6 months as recession hit
- **Hedge:** Diversify across oil longs and recession hedges (TLT, gold)

### Risk 4: China retaliates with trade measures

- **Probability:** 15-25%
- **Trigger:** China sees blockade as escalation against its energy security; retaliates via tariffs, Treasury sales, or sanctions evasion
- **Market impact:**
  - If China sells US Treasuries → US yields spike, USD volatility
  - If China organizes sanctions-busting fleet → blockade effectiveness undermined
  - Trade war escalation → global growth hit → oil demand down
- **Historical: ** China has been building strategic petroleum reserves (~1B+ barrels); can absorb short-term disruption

### Risk 5: Strait of Hormuz escalation

- **Probability:** 10-15% (but catastrophic if realized)
- **Trigger:** Iran retaliates against blockade by threatening/mining the Strait
- **Market impact:** ~20M bbl/day flows through Hormuz (~20% of global supply); closure = oil $150-200+
- **Why it matters:** Even a *threat* of closure adds $10-20/bbl risk premium
- **Hedge:** Out-of-the-money calls on oil serve as tail-risk protection

### Risk 6: US domestic political backlash

- **Probability:** 30-40%
- **Trigger:** Higher gasoline prices ($4-5+/gallon) → voter anger → political pressure to ease blockade
- **Market impact:** Policy reversal → oil drops sharply
- **Mitigant:** US could release SPR (Strategic Petroleum Reserve, currently ~370M barrels) to suppress domestic prices
