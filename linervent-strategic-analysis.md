# Strategic Analysis: Linervent Partnership & FLEX Liner Timeline

**Prepared by:** CSO, SacPro Group
**Date:** February 2026
**Classification:** Internal — Management Discussion

---

## 1. Current Situation

SacPro Group faces a compounding strategic problem involving our delayed in-house liner (FLEX), our underperforming resale agreement with Linervent, and growing competitive exposure in our core markets.

### The FLEX Liner (Our Own Product)
- Felt liner, designed to be a **superior product** in the market.
- Development is approximately **18 months behind schedule**.
- Delays are driven by the ambition to create a technically superior liner, which has introduced production complications.
- No confirmed delivery date at this time.

### The Linervent WO Liner (Current Resale Product)
- Polyester woven liner purchased from Linervent (Sweden) and sold under the brand **House Liner WO**.
- **Poor market reception** — customers do not prefer it, and internal staff lack confidence in the product.
- We are **not meeting contractual purchase volumes**, causing **stock to accumulate monthly**.
- We do sell some volume to international resellers, but far below contractual commitments.

### Linervent's New Felt Liner
- Linervent has now developed **their own felt liner** and offered us the option to resell it.
- It is positioned as a **lower-cost, budget product** — not top-tier.
- Risk: if priced aggressively, it could **compress liner prices across Scandinavia**.

### Channel Conflict
- Linervent **already sells directly to resellers** in the same geographic areas where SacPro operates.
- They sell to those resellers at **the same price they sell to us**, making it impossible for our resellers to compete — they have one additional margin layer that Linervent's direct customers do not.
- This is an ongoing issue that undermines our distribution model.

## 3. Strategic Options

### Option A: Terminate the Linervent Contract

**Action:** End the resale agreement for the WO liner. Do not take on their felt liner.

**Consequences:**
- Linervent will sell **directly to our customers and resellers** at the price we currently buy at — they become an immediate, aggressive competitor.
- We lose access to any liner product to offer customers until FLEX is ready.
- We are left with **no competitive product in the market** for an undefined period.
- Existing WO stock must be liquidated, likely at a loss.
- Our customers who do buy WO will switch directly to Linervent.

**Risk level:** Very high
**When it makes sense:** Only if FLEX is weeks away from market-ready and we are confident in the timeline.

---

### Option B: Maintain the WO Contract As-Is

**Action:** Continue buying and reselling the WO liner. Decline the felt liner offer.

**Consequences:**
- Stock continues to grow every month as sales remain below contractual volumes.
- Linervent launches their felt liner and **sells it directly** in our markets, including Scandinavia.
- They undercut felt liner pricing, potentially damaging the price environment for FLEX before it even launches.
- We remain stuck with a product our customers and staff don't believe in.
- We continue to fund Linervent's growth by meeting (or trying to meet) purchase commitments.

**Risk level:** High — we bleed cash on inventory while Linervent captures felt liner market share.
**When it makes sense:** If we believe FLEX is very close and we just need to hold position for a short defined period.

---

### Option C: Switch from WO to Linervent's Felt Liner

**Action:** Negotiate a transition from reselling the WO liner to reselling Linervent's felt liner instead.

**Consequences:**
- We gain a **felt liner product to offer immediately**, even though it is not our own.
- Reduces urgency on FLEX timeline — buys us time.
- However, we become **more dependent on Linervent**, not less.
- Their felt liner is a budget product — selling it under our brand may **damage SacPro's premium positioning**.
- If/when FLEX launches, we then need to migrate customers from a Linervent felt liner to our own felt liner — this could be messy.
- Linervent gains deeper insight into our customer base and pricing.
- The channel conflict problem **remains unchanged** — they still sell at our buy price to competing resellers.

**Risk level:** Medium-high — solves the short-term product gap but deepens strategic dependency.
**When it makes sense:** If FLEX is realistically 6-12+ months away and we cannot afford to have no felt liner in the market.

---

### Option D: Take the Felt Liner AND Set a Hard FLEX Deadline

**Action:** Accept Linervent's felt liner as a bridge product. Simultaneously set an internal hard deadline for FLEX (e.g., Q3 2026) with consequences if missed.

**Consequences:**
- We have a product to sell now — stops the bleeding in the short term.
- FLEX team gets a non-negotiable deadline, which may force difficult trade-offs on product spec (good — perfect is the enemy of done).
- We accept a period of Linervent dependency with a **defined exit date**.
- Requires honest internal assessment: is FLEX delayed because it's genuinely hard, or because there is no forcing function?
- If FLEX misses the hard deadline again, we are in a worse position — deeper into Linervent dependency with no exit.

**Risk level:** Medium — best balance of short-term survival and long-term independence, but depends entirely on FLEX delivery.
**When it makes sense:** If management is willing to enforce a hard deadline and accept that FLEX may launch as "very good" rather than "perfect."

---

### Option E: Negotiate Exclusivity / Territory Protection with Linervent

**Action:** Before making any product decision, renegotiate the Linervent agreement to include **geographic exclusivity** or **price differentiation** for SacPro territories.

**Consequences:**
- If successful, solves the channel conflict — our resellers can actually compete.
- Gives us a viable business model for reselling any Linervent product (WO or felt).
- **Linervent has little incentive to agree** — they benefit from the current arrangement where they sell to everyone.
- May require us to commit to higher volumes to earn exclusivity, which is the opposite of our current trajectory.
- Even if agreed, enforcing territory protections is difficult.

**Risk level:** Medium — worth attempting, but low probability of success unless we bring significant leverage.
**When it makes sense:** As a negotiation attempt before committing to any other option. Even partial success (price differentiation) helps.

---

## 4. The Fundamental Dilemma

Every option carries the same underlying tension:

> **We do not have our own product ready. Until we do, every decision is a trade-off between short-term survival and long-term strategic independence.**

- If we stay close to Linervent, we survive but become more dependent and fund a competitor.
- If we distance ourselves from Linervent, we lose market presence until FLEX arrives — and we don't know when that is.

The **single most important variable** in this entire analysis is: **when will FLEX actually be ready?**

Without a credible, hard answer to that question, every other decision is made in the dark.

---

## 5. Questions for Management Discussion

1. **FLEX timeline** — Can production give us a date they will commit to, with accountability? What needs to be descoped from FLEX to hit a hard deadline?

2. **Contractual exposure** — What are the financial penalties or obligations if we terminate or renegotiate the Linervent contract? What does the contract actually require?

3. **WO inventory** — What is the current stock value, and what is the monthly carrying cost? Can any of it be liquidated at discount to clear the position?

4. **Linervent felt liner terms** — What price, volumes, and conditions are they proposing? Would switching from WO to felt reduce our minimum commitments?

5. **Channel conflict documentation** — Do we have evidence of Linervent undercutting our resellers? This could be leverage in renegotiation.

6. **Risk tolerance** — Is management willing to accept a period with no liner product in the market if it means cutting Linervent loose? How long?

7. **FLEX spec** — Is the team willing to launch a "good enough" FLEX first and iterate, rather than waiting for the superior version? A phased launch could change every timeline in this analysis.

---

## 6. Recommended Path Forward (For Discussion)

A combined approach, executed in sequence:

1. **Immediately** — Demand an honest, accountable FLEX delivery date from production. Not a target — a commitment with defined consequences.

2. **In parallel** — Open renegotiation with Linervent on territory protection and pricing. Test what's possible before making product decisions.

3. **If FLEX is < 6 months away** — Maintain WO contract, decline felt liner, manage stock. Accept short-term pain for strategic independence.

4. **If FLEX is > 6 months away** — Seriously consider taking Linervent's felt liner as a bridge, but only with better commercial terms (pricing, territory, reduced minimums).

5. **Regardless** — Begin planning WO stock liquidation. This inventory is a sunk cost that grows every month.
