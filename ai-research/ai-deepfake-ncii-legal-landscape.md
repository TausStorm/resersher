# AI-Generated Non-Consensual Pornography: Global Legal & Regulatory Landscape

*Research compiled May 2026*

---

## 1. US Federal Laws

### TAKE IT DOWN Act (Pub. L. 119-12) -- ENACTED
- **Signed:** May 19, 2025 by President Trump
- **Sponsors:** Sen. Ted Cruz (R-TX), Sen. Amy Klobuchar (D-MN)
- **Vote:** Senate unanimous consent; House 409-2
- **Key provisions:**
  - **Criminalizes** publication of nonconsensual intimate images (NCII), both real and AI-generated
  - Up to **2 years imprisonment**, harsher penalties for minors
  - Requires platforms to **remove flagged NCII within 48 hours** and delete copies
  - Enforcement by the **Federal Trade Commission (FTC)**
  - Criminal provisions effective immediately; platform takedown systems required **1 year after passage** (May 19, 2026)
- **First conviction:** April 2026 -- Ohio man used AI to create NCII of adults and children, shared on CSAM site
- **Criticisms:**
  - EFF, CDT, Freedom of the Press Foundation warn of vague language enabling censorship of legal content
  - Takedown system vulnerable to bad-faith abuse (DMCA-style problems)
  - May require breaking **end-to-end encryption** to comply with takedown notices
  - Trump's firing of Democratic FTC commissioners raises **selective enforcement** concerns
  - Cyber Civil Rights Initiative: platforms aligned with administration may "simply ignore reports"

**Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TAKE_IT_DOWN_Act | https://www.theverge.com/news/657632/take-it-down-act-passes-house-deepfakes

### DEFIANCE Act -- PASSED SENATE (Jan 2026)
- **Full name:** Disrupt Explicit Forged Images and Non-Consensual Edits Act
- **Sponsor:** Rep. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez
- **Key provision:** Provides victims **civil right to sue** creators/distributors of AI-generated NCII for damages
- **Status:** Passed Senate by unanimous consent July 2024, failed in House. Reintroduced May 2025. **Senate passed again January 2026** -- awaiting House action
- **Distinct from TAKE IT DOWN:** Criminal vs. civil remedy. DEFIANCE gives victims private cause of action; TAKE IT DOWN criminalizes the conduct and creates platform obligations

**Source:** https://www.politico.com/live-updates/2026/01/13/congress/deepfake-porn-bill-allowing-victims-to-sue-passes-senate-00725817

### SHIELD Act -- PENDING
- **Full name:** Stopping Harmful Image Exploitation and Limiting Distribution Act
- **Sponsor:** Sen. Klobuchar, Sen. Cornyn
- **Key provision:** Bolsters law enforcement tools to investigate/charge NCII publishers, harsher penalties for minors
- **Status:** Passed Senate 2024, failed House. Reintroduced February 2025

### Other Federal Efforts
- **Deepfake Accountability Act** -- introduced 2019, died 2020. Would have required watermarks on deepfakes
- **Revised version** introduced 2021 requiring audiovisual disclosure of alterations -- died 2023
- No comprehensive federal AI-specific deepfake regulation beyond TAKE IT DOWN

## 2. US State Laws

### Overview
As of 2025, **48+ states** have laws addressing nonconsensual intimate imagery in some form. At least **38 states** have laws specifically addressing AI-generated CSAM. Key variations:

### Early Movers on AI/Deepfake-Specific Laws
| State | Key Provision | Notes |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| **California** | Illegal to disseminate AI-generated pornographic images without consent | Explicit deepfake provision |
| **Virginia** | Criminal ban on sharing AI-generated intimate images without consent | Among first states |
| **New York** | Criminalizes dissemination of AI-generated pornographic images | Explicit deepfake provision |
| **Texas** | Laws on deepfake videos (not originally photos, which prompted the TAKE IT DOWN Act) | Gap revealed by Aledo incident |
| **Minnesota** | Criminal penalties for deepfake intimate images | Early adopter |
| **Georgia** | AI-generated NCII provisions | |
| **Florida** | Deepfake intimate imagery provisions | |
| **Illinois** | Civil cause of action for deepfake pornography | |

### Key Variations Across States
- **Criminal vs. civil:** Some states only provide civil remedies (lawsuits); others criminalize
- **AI-specific vs. general NCII:** Some laws explicitly cover AI/deepfake; others rely on broader NCII statutes that may or may not cover synthetic imagery
- **"Intent" requirements:** Some require proof of intent to harm, harass, or intimidate; others don't
- **Minor-specific provisions:** Most have enhanced penalties for minors
- **"Student-on-student" gap:** As of 2025, no clear framework in most states for handling AI-generated CSAM created by minors targeting other minors in school settings (per 404 Media reporting)

### Notable Gap (Massachusetts)
Massachusetts was historically the last state without any revenge porn law -- only recently passing one.

## 3. EU & UK Regulations

### EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) -- IN FORCE
- **Entered into force:** August 1, 2024, with phased implementation over 6-36 months
- **Deepfake provisions (Limited Risk category):**
  - AI systems generating/manipulating images, audio, or video (including deepfakes) classified as **"limited risk"** with **transparency obligations**
  - Users must be **informed they are interacting with AI-generated content**
  - **Does NOT specifically ban deepfake NCII** -- focuses on transparency/labeling
  - Legal scholars argue deepfake NCII should be "high risk" requiring stricter regulation, but the Act doesn't go that far
  - No explicit mandate for deepfake detection mechanisms
- **Penalties:** Up to EUR 35M or 7% global turnover for prohibited AI; up to EUR 15M or 3% for other violations
- **Criticism:** Amnesty International says it "fails to take basic human rights principles into account." Civil society groups say loopholes favor big tech

**Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_Intelligence_Act

### UK Online Safety Act 2023 -- IN FORCE
- **Royal assent:** October 26, 2023
- **Key provisions on intimate images:**
  - Amends Sexual Offences Act 2003 to **criminalize sharing** intimate images that show or **"appear to show"** another person (thus covering deepfakes) **without consent**
  - Creates duty of care for platforms to remove illegal content
  - Platforms face fines up to **GBP 18M or 10% annual turnover**
  - Enforcement by **Ofcom**
  - Age verification requirements for pornographic sites (effective July 25, 2025)
- **2024 Criminal Justice Bill:** Would have added criminalization of **creating** deepfake pornographic images (not just sharing) -- bill died when Parliament dissolved for election

### UK Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 -- ENACTED
- Created new offence: **creating or requesting creation** of a "purported intimate image of another person" without explicit consent
- Closes the gap from the Online Safety Act which only criminalized sharing, not creating

**Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Online_Safety_Act_2023 | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deepfake_pornography

### EU Digital Services Act (DSA)
- Requires large platforms to assess and mitigate systemic risks including NCII
- Platforms must provide mechanisms for users to flag illegal content
- Complementary to AI Act but not deepfake-specific

## 4. Other Countries

### South Korea -- STRONGEST LAWS GLOBALLY
- **Existing law:** Creation, distribution, or possession of deepfake pornography classified as a **sex crime**
- **Mandatory prison sentence:** 3-7 years under Special Act on Sexual Violence Crimes
- **September 2024 amendment:** Following massive Telegram deepfake scandal (220,000+ member groups targeting schoolgirls):
  - **Possession or viewing** of sexually explicit deepfakes criminalized
  - Up to **3 years prison or 30M won (~$22,600) fine** for buying/saving/watching
  - Creating for distribution: penalty increased from 5 to **7 years**, regardless of intent
- **Scale:** 800+ deepfake sex crime cases by September 2024 (up from 156 in 2021), most victims/offenders teenagers
- **September 21, 2024:** 6,000 protesters gathered in Seoul demanding stronger enforcement

**Source:** https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deepfake_pornography

### Australia
- **Criminal Code (Cth):** Non-consensual sharing of intimate images -- up to 5 years imprisonment (since 2018)
- **All states/territories** have complementary laws (penalties vary: typically 2-3 years)
- **First AI-specific prosecution:** April 2025 -- William Hamish Yeates (19) pleaded guilty to creating/distributing deepfake pornography, first person charged under new national laws targeting AI-manipulated sexual images
- **Online Safety Amendment 2024** strengthens provisions further
- **Civil penalties scheme** operates alongside criminal law

### Japan
- **2014 law:** Crime to distribute "private sexual image of another person" without consent
- No specific AI/deepfake legislation yet, though general revenge porn laws may apply
- Enforcement gaps for AI-generated content

### China
- **Deep synthesis regulations (2023):** Providers of "deep synthesis" technology (including deepfakes) must:
  - Label AI-generated content
  - Not use technology to produce content prohibited by law (which includes pornography)
  - Obtain consent for use of personal likenesses
- **Cyberspace Administration of China** issued specific regulations on generative AI (effective August 2023)
- Pornography broadly illegal, so AI-generated pornography falls under existing obscenity laws
- In practice, enforcement focuses more on political/social stability than individual victim rights

### Germany
- Justice Minister Stefanie Hubig preparing dedicated **anti-deepfake pornography law**
- Will extend police authority to search devices and improve offender identification
- High-profile case: TV presenter Collien Fernandes filed complaint against ex-husband for creating fake nude content over years

## 5. Platform Policies

### Meta (Facebook, Instagram)
- Bans non-consensual intimate imagery (both real and AI-generated)
- Automated detection tools for known NCII
- Partnership with StopNCII.org for hash-matching
- Removed Taylor Swift deepfakes from Facebook/Instagram

### Google
- Since 2018: "involuntary synthetic pornographic imagery" on ban list
- Individuals can request removal of such content from search results
- Google's president of global affairs called TAKE IT DOWN Act passage "a big step toward protecting individuals"

### X (formerly Twitter)
- Policy bans non-consensual nudity
- Temporarily disabled searches for "Taylor Swift" during January 2024 deepfake crisis
- **Grok scandal (Dec 2025-Jan 2026):** xAI's Grok chatbot found to comply with requests to "undress" individuals including minors. Analysis: 6,700 sexually suggestive/nudified images generated per hour. xAI responded to media inquiries with "Legacy Media Lies"
- International backlash from EU, UK, Poland, France, India, Malaysia, Brazil

### Telegram
- Major vector for deepfake NCII distribution
- South Korean Telegram scandal (2024): groups with 220,000+ members creating/sharing deepfakes of schoolgirls
- "Nudify" bots on Telegram reached ~4 million monthly users by October 2024
- Taylor Swift deepfakes in January 2024 originated from Telegram groups
- Platform criticized for insufficient moderation

### Reddit, Discord, Snapchat
- All banned deepfake pornographic content (Reddit since 2018)
- Reddit implementing age verification under UK Online Safety Act (via Persona)
- Snap "applauded" TAKE IT DOWN Act passage

### Pornhub / MindGeek
- Banned deepfake content since 2018
- Content moderation challenged by volume

### App Stores (Apple, Google Play)
- Major storefronts have implemented stricter policies and automated detection to ban "nudification" apps
- Proliferation shifted to decentralized websites, forums, and third-party messaging bot ecosystems

## 6. Gaps in Current Law & Enforcement Challenges

### Major Gaps

1. **No comprehensive federal US civil remedy (yet):** DEFIANCE Act still pending in House. TAKE IT DOWN provides criminal penalties but limited civil recourse for victims
2. **Encryption conflict:** TAKE IT DOWN may require platforms to break end-to-end encryption to scan for/remove NCII -- technically infeasible without undermining privacy
3. **Student-on-student AI CSAM:** No clear legal framework in most states for handling minors creating AI-generated NCII of other minors in school settings
4. **EU AI Act only requires transparency, not prohibition:** Deepfake NCII classified as "limited risk" (transparency obligations) rather than "high risk" or banned
5. **Cross-border enforcement:** NCII creators often in different jurisdictions from victims and platforms. Telegram (UAE-based), offshore sites beyond reach of national laws
6. **Open-source AI models:** Once AI models are released openly, preventing their use for NCII is essentially impossible at the technology level
7. **Detection arms race:** Detection tools (e.g., DeepRhythm at 98% accuracy) lag behind generation capabilities; no reliable at-scale detection mandated by law
8. **Selective enforcement risk:** FTC political dynamics under Trump (fired Democratic commissioners) create fear of partisan enforcement of TAKE IT DOWN
9. **Bad-faith takedown abuse:** TAKE IT DOWN's notice system lacks robust counter-notice mechanism (unlike DMCA), vulnerable to weaponization
10. **Volume overwhelm:** Estimated millions of AI-generated NCII images created monthly; 48-hour takedown window may be unrealistic at scale

### Enforcement Challenges

- **Anonymity:** Creators often anonymous, using VPNs, offshore platforms
- **Whack-a-mole:** Content removed from one platform resurfaces elsewhere
- **Victim identification burden:** Victims must find and report content, often across dozens of platforms
- **Jurisdictional fragmentation:** 48+ state laws with different standards create compliance confusion
- **Resource constraints:** Law enforcement often lacks technical capacity for deepfake cases
- **Social stigma:** Many victims don't report due to shame, compounding the problem

### What's Still Missing Globally

| Gap | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| **Universal creation ban** | Few countries criminalize the mere *creation* (vs. distribution) of deepfake NCII. UK's Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 is rare exception |
| **AI model accountability** | No major jurisdiction holds AI tool developers liable for NCII created with their tools |
| **Mandatory detection** | No law requires platforms to proactively detect (vs. react to reports of) deepfake NCII |
| **International treaty** | No multilateral agreement on deepfake NCII, despite cross-border nature |
| **Victim support infrastructure** | Limited funded resources for victims navigating multi-platform, multi-jurisdiction takedowns |
