# The Lure — Narrative Framework

## Concept

A realistic, awareness-raising story that follows **multiple girls** from different backgrounds who are drawn into exploitation through different entry points — showing that it's not stupidity, it's sophisticated manipulation targeting normal human needs. The story also follows **the people trying to stop it**: a school counselor, a detective, a survivor-turned-advocate.

The power of this story: **the audience should recognize the tactics**, not just from "bad neighborhoods" but from their own kids' phones, their own social circles, their own blind spots.

---

## Part 1: How It Actually Happens

### The Core Truth Most People Miss

Exploitation doesn't start with force. It starts with **meeting a need the girl already has**. That's what makes it so effective and so hard to spot.

Every tactic below exploits a legitimate human need:
- Need for love and validation
- Need for money/financial independence  
- Need for belonging and identity
- Need to escape a bad situation
- Need for excitement and status

### Realistic Character Paths (Composite Profiles Based on Documented Patterns)

---

#### **Sara, 14 — The Loverboy Method**

**Background:** Divorced parents. Mom works double shifts. Sara feels invisible at home and average at school. Not "troubled" — just lonely.

**How it happens:**
1. **Identification** — A guy (19-22) notices her at a mall/bus stop. Or more commonly now: on Instagram/TikTok/Snapchat. He's attractive, confident, older.
2. **Love-bombing** — He texts constantly. "You're not like other girls." "I've never felt this way." He remembers everything she says. He makes her feel *seen* for the first time.
3. **Isolation** — Subtle at first. "Your friends don't really get you." "Your mom doesn't appreciate you." He becomes her world. She pulls away from her support network voluntarily.
4. **Testing boundaries** — Asks for photos. "Just for me." If she hesitates: "Don't you trust me?" Introduces alcohol/drugs casually.
5. **The trap** — "I'm in trouble, I owe money." "If you loved me you'd help." Or: he's already shared her photos. Now there's leverage. "Just this once, with my friend."
6. **Normalization** — By now her reality is distorted. She may not even frame it as exploitation. He's her boyfriend. She's "helping."

**Why it works:** Sara isn't naive. The loverboy method works on adults too. It's the same psychology as domestic abuse — trauma bonding, intermittent reinforcement, isolation from support.

**Time frame:** 3-6 months from first contact to exploitation. Sometimes faster.

---

#### **Amira, 16 — The Opportunity Trap**

**Background:** Immigrant family, financial pressure. Amira is ambitious, wants to help her parents. Good student.

**How it happens:**
1. **The offer** — A "modeling scout" approaches her. Or: a job offer on social media — hostessing, promotional work, good pay. Or: someone from her community offers "work abroad."
2. **Legitimacy theater** — A website. Business cards. A "studio." Maybe even a first payment. Everything *looks* real.
3. **Escalation** — The first job is fine. Second job: "The client wants something different." Pressure increases. "You already accepted the money." "Think about your family."
4. **Debt bondage** — "We paid for your travel/accommodation/photos. You owe us." The debt never shrinks.
5. **Fear** — If undocumented: "We'll call immigration." If documented: "We have photos. We know where your family lives."

**Why it works:** Amira is making a rational decision with incomplete information. The exploiters invest in looking legitimate specifically to pass due diligence.

---

#### **Elin, 15 — The Social Media Pipeline**

**Background:** Stable home, two parents, middle-class. Elin is insecure about her body and seeks validation online.

**How it happens:**
1. **Attention economy** — She posts on Instagram/TikTok. Followers grow. DMs come in. Most are harmless. Some aren't.
2. **Gradual normalization** — "You should have an OnlyFans when you turn 18." "You'd make so much money." She's told her value = her appearance. This comes from *culture*, not just predators.
3. **The groomer** — One DM is different. Patient. Flattering. Doesn't ask for anything for weeks. Builds trust.
4. **The shift** — Requests for private photos "just between us." Introduction to "sugar daddy" culture framed as empowerment. "It's your body, your choice" — weaponizing feminist language.
5. **Entrapment** — Material exists. It's shared. Blackmail. Or: she's introduced to "clients" through the person she trusted.

**Why it works:** The line between mainstream social media culture (validation through appearance, monetizing attention) and exploitation is deliberately blurred by predators. Elin can't see where one ends and the other begins because culturally, neither can we.

---

#### **Josefin, 13 — The Gaming/Discord Path**

**Background:** Introverted, few friends at school, active in online gaming communities.

**How it happens:**
1. **Community** — She finds belonging in a Discord server or gaming community. An older member (or someone pretending to be her age) befriends her.
2. **Private channels** — The friendship moves to DMs. He's her best friend. He "gets her."
3. **Normalization of sexual content** — Sharing memes that become increasingly explicit. "Everyone does this." Group pressure in private servers.
4. **Requests** — Escalating from conversation to photos to video. Always framed as normal, mutual, playful.
5. **Distribution** — Content is shared in networks she never knew existed. Used for CSAM. Or: used as leverage.

**Why it works:** Parents don't monitor Discord/gaming the way they monitor Instagram. The predator operates in a space adults don't understand or see.

## Part 2: The Systemic Failures

### Why We Keep Failing to Prevent This

**1. The "Perfect Victim" Myth**
Society imagines trafficking victims as kidnapped, chained, foreign. Reality: most are domestic, never physically restrained, and may not identify as victims. **When the girl walks in "willingly," systems fail to see her.**

**2. The Shame Architecture**
The moment a girl is exploited, shame becomes the wall that keeps her silent:
- "I sent those photos myself"
- "I went with him willingly" 
- "My parents would die if they knew"
- "No one will believe me"
- "I'll get in trouble too"

Every one of these is by design. Exploiters construct situations where the victim feels complicit.

**3. The Technology Gap**
- Parents don't know what Discord, Snapchat disappearing messages, or finsta accounts are
- Schools teach "stranger danger" — but the threat is someone who feels like a best friend or boyfriend
- Law enforcement struggles with encrypted platforms and jurisdictional issues

**4. The Social Norm Pipeline**
Mainstream culture normalizes:
- Valuing girls for appearance above all
- "Older boyfriend" as status symbol
- Secrecy in relationships as romantic
- Sugar dating as empowerment
- Hypersexualized content for younger audiences

Predators don't have to build the whole road. Culture builds most of it. They just open the last door.

**5. Under-Resourced Response**
- School counselors handle 500+ students
- Police units for exploitation are tiny
- Social services are overloaded
- Survivors wait months for therapy

## Part 3: Prevention — What Actually Works

### What Doesn't Work
- Scare tactics ("this will happen to you") — creates fear, not skills
- Abstinence from technology — unrealistic, creates secrecy
- "Just say no" messaging — ignores the psychology of grooming
- Blaming victims — ensures silence

### What Works

**1. Teaching the Pattern, Not Just the Danger**

Instead of "don't talk to strangers," teach kids to recognize:
- **Love-bombing** — when someone makes you feel special too fast, that's a flag
- **Isolation tactics** — "don't tell anyone about us" is never romantic
- **Escalation** — small asks that get bigger are a manipulation pattern
- **Guilt/debt** — "after everything I've done for you" is control, not love

Make this part of normal education from age 11-12. Not a scary assembly — an ongoing conversation.

**2. Building Critical Digital Literacy**

- How to verify if a person/opportunity is real
- Understanding that screenshots exist even on "disappearing" platforms
- Recognizing fake profiles and social engineering
- Knowing that sextortion is a crime committed *against* you, not *by* you

**3. Strengthening the Safety Net**

The #1 protective factor is **having at least one trusted adult**. Prevention must:
- Train teachers, coaches, youth leaders to spot warning signs (sudden behavioral changes, new expensive items, secretive phone use, older "boyfriend")
- Create low-barrier reporting — anonymous tip lines, school-based drop-in
- Remove shame from the equation: "Nothing you've done means you can't get help"

**4. Addressing Vulnerability at the Root**

Girls enter exploitation because a need isn't met. Prevention means:
- Stable housing and economic security for families
- Mental health support in schools
- Mentorship and belonging programs
- Addressing loneliness as the crisis it is

**5. Going After Demand**

The story should show: without buyers, there is no market. The Swedish/Nordic Model criminalizes buying, not selling. Prevention includes:
- Holding buyers accountable
- Platform accountability for enabling exploitation
- Financial tracking of trafficking networks

**6. Survivor-Led Solutions**

The most effective prevention programs are designed by survivors. They know the language, the entry points, the moments where intervention could have worked.

## Part 4: Story Structure Suggestion

### Dual Timeline / Interweaving Structure

**Thread A — The Girls**
Follow Sara, Amira, and Elin over 6-8 months. Show the grooming process in painful, realistic detail. The audience sees what the girls can't: the pattern. Each girl's story converges at a crisis point.

**Thread B — The Responders**
- **Maria** — school counselor who notices something off about Sara but has 600 students and no time
- **Henrik** — detective in an underfunded exploitation unit, tracing a network online
- **Fatima** — survivor, now 25, running a peer support organization, burning out

**Thread C — The Blindspot**
Show the parents, friends, teachers who *almost* see it. The text message they almost read. The question they almost asked. The moment they let it go because "she seems fine" or "it's probably just a phase."

### Tone
Not sensational. Not voyeuristic. Clinical where needed, emotional where it matters. The exploitation is never shown for shock value — it's shown to make the audience understand the *mechanism*.

**The most disturbing thing in this story should be how normal everything looks from the outside.**

### Ending
Not fully resolved. Some girls are helped. Some aren't found in time. The systems improve slightly but remain underfunded. The survivor keeps going. The counselor learns to ask the question she was afraid to ask.

The message: **this is preventable, but only if we stop looking away.**

## Part 5: Research Anchors for Realism

To make this as realistic as possible, ground it in:

1. **The "Loverboy" / "Romeo pimp" method** — extensively documented in Netherlands, Sweden, UK, US. Academic literature + NGO reports from ECPAT, Polaris Project, National Center for Missing & Exploited Children.

2. **Survivor testimonies** — books like *Girls Like Us* (Rachel Lloyd), *Sold* (Patricia McCormick), *The Slave Across the Street* (Theresa Flores). Documentaries: *I Am Jane Doe*, *Tricked*, *Selling the Girl Next Door*.

3. **Swedish context** — reports from Barnahus, ECPAT Sweden, Stiftelsen Allmänna Barnhuset, Jämställdhetsmyndigheten (Gender Equality Agency). The Nordic Model debate.

4. **Digital exploitation** — Internet Watch Foundation data, Thorn reports on online grooming, FBI sextortion warnings (2023 surge in cases targeting minors).

5. **Prevention science** — what evidence-based programs exist (My Body My Life, Steered Straight, survivor-led programs). What school-based interventions show results.
