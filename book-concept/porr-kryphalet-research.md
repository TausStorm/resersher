# "Porrinspelningen" — The Porn Loophole

## Research for book/film: How the legal gray area between pornography and prostitution is exploited in Sweden

---

## The Paradox in Swedish Law

Two laws sit side by side in direct tension:

1. **Sexköpslagen (1999, BrB 6:11)** — Buying sex is a crime. Since 2022, fines removed; now only prison up to 1 year. Since July 2025, expanded to cover webcam/distance sexual acts too.

2. **Pornography is legal (since 1971)** — Sweden was one of the first countries to legalize pornography. There are *no regulations* on adult pornography production. No licensing. No registration. No mandatory record-keeping of performers. No industry body.

**The paradox**, as professor Mariah Larsson (film studies) has publicly pointed out: paying someone to have sex with you = crime. Paying someone to have sex with you *while a camera is rolling* = legal media production.

The 2025 expansion of sexköpslagen explicitly exempts **pre-recorded material** (i.e., pornography). So the loophole didn't just survive the latest reform — it was written into it.

## The Scenario: "Casting Parties"

### How It Works in Practice

The setup described — and documented through Swedish media reports and NGO work — looks like this:

1. **An organizer** (always male, usually 30-50) arranges a "porn shoot" or "casting"
2. **Young women** (18-25) are recruited as "models" or "performers"
3. **The "shoot"** is actually a party at a rented location — villa, hotel suite, Airbnb
4. **Multiple men** attend as "actors" or "crew" (often just paying guests)
5. **Cameras are present** — phones, GoPros, sometimes a proper camera — providing legal cover
6. **The women are paid** for their "performance" — typically 3,000–10,000 SEK per session
7. **The material may never be published** — the filming is the alibi, not the product

From a legal standpoint, the organizer can claim: *"This was a pornographic production. The women were compensated performers. The men were actors. The cameras prove it."*

This makes it nearly impossible to prosecute under sexköpslagen, because the legal distinction between "buying sex" and "producing pornography" collapses when a camera is present.

## Question 1: How Do They Announce Castings While Staying Anonymous?

### The Anonymity Architecture

These operations use layered anonymity at every step:

#### A. Pseudonyms / Producer Names
- The organizer never uses their real name
- They operate under a "producer alias" — e.g., "Studio X" or "Nordic Productions" or just a first name like "Alex"
- No registered company. No org number (organisationsnummer). No public records
- Sometimes a minimalist website exists (one-page, no address, no names, hosted abroad)

#### B. Burner Communication
- **Prepaid SIM cards** (kontantkort) — still somewhat available in Sweden, though registration requirements have tightened
- **Encrypted messaging apps** — particularly **Telegram** (pseudonymous accounts, self-destructing messages, channels that can be created anonymously)
- **ProtonMail / Tutanota** — anonymous email services
- **VPN** — masks IP address when posting ads

#### C. Cash and Crypto
- Performers paid in cash (no bank trail)
- Or via Revolut / crypto / other hard-to-trace methods
- No invoices, no contracts (or fake ones)
- If Swish is used, it's from a prepaid or secondary account

#### D. Rented Locations
- Airbnb or short-term rentals under a fake or borrowed identity
- Sometimes hotel suites booked through intermediaries
- Locations change each time — no fixed studio = no fixed address to investigate
- Sometimes outside Sweden entirely (Tallinn, Riga, Malaga — filmed as "international production")

#### E. No Paper Trail of Actual Publication
- The key trick: the "content" doesn't need to be published anywhere
- If police ask "where is this pornography distributed?" — there's no website, no platform, no distribution deal
- The organizer can claim the production is "unreleased," "in editing," or "for private distribution"
- Some content may end up on amateur sites, hidden behind accounts, but tracing it back is nearly impossible

## Question 2: What Platforms Are Used to Recruit?

This is the most important part for prevention. The recruitment happens across several layers:

### Tier 1: Open Platforms (Initial Contact / Scouting)

| Platform | How It's Used | Warning Signs |
|----------|--------------|---------------|
| **Instagram** | Organizer follows/DMs young women who post modeling-type photos. "We're looking for models for a paid shoot." Vague at first. | DM from unknown "producer" with no verifiable company. Offers money for "modeling." Profile has few posts, mostly reposts. |
| **TikTok** | Same approach. Comments on videos: "You should model." DMs follow. | Unsolicited "talent scouting" from anonymous accounts. |
| **Snapchat** | Used as second step after initial IG/TikTok contact. Conversations move here because messages disappear. | Being asked to move conversation to Snapchat "for privacy." |
| **Facebook Groups** | Modeling groups, "tjäna pengar snabbt" (earn money fast) groups, student groups. Posts offering "well-paid photo/video work." | Anonymous posters in modeling groups. No company info. Unusually high pay offered. |

### Tier 2: Gray-Area Platforms (Active Recruitment)

| Platform | How It's Used | Warning Signs |
|----------|--------------|---------------|
| **Telegram** | **Primary platform.** Anonymous channels and groups dedicated to "casting calls" or "content creation opportunities." Some are invite-only. Organizers post looking for "models" with specific descriptions. | Any Telegram "casting call" group. No real names. Requests for photos before meeting. |
| **Sugar Dating Sites** (Seeking, SugarDaters, etc.) | Used as recruitment pools. Women already on these platforms are offered "something better paid" — framed as a step up. "It's not sugar dating, it's professional modeling." | Someone on a sugar site pivoting to "Why don't you do some paid work for me instead?" |
| **OnlyFans / Fansly "Collabs"** | Organizer approaches OF creators proposing "collaboration content." Legitimate in the industry — but used as cover to recruit for party-format shoots. | "Collab" proposals from people with no visible content. Vague about what the shoot involves. |
| **Adult Classified Sites** | Various international sites where "casting calls" are posted. Sometimes Swedish-specific escort forums where the framing shifts from "escort work" to "production work." | Any online ad seeking "open-minded models" for "private productions." |
| **Discord** | Some recruitment through invite-only servers, particularly in communities adjacent to adult content creation. | Private servers with "casting" or "content creation" channels. |

### Tier 3: Offline / Word of Mouth

| Method | How It's Used |
|--------|--------------|
| **Existing performers recruit friends** | A woman who has done one shoot is offered extra money to bring a friend. Peer recruitment lowers suspicion. |
| **Nightclub/bar scouting** | Organizer or his "recruiter" approaches women in nightlife settings. Business card with just a phone number. |
| **Student networks** | Rumors circulate about "easy money." A friend of a friend knows someone. No one knows the organizer's real name. |

### The Recruitment Script (Typical Pattern)

1. **First contact**: Flattering, professional tone. "We're a small production company looking for new faces."
2. **The pitch**: "We do artistic/tasteful content. You control what happens. It's safe and well-paid."
3. **Gradual escalation**: First mention of "adult content." Then "intimate scenes." Then details of what's expected.
4. **Normalization**: "Lots of girls do this. It's totally legal. You'll be with other girls your age. It's like a party."
5. **Financial hook**: Amounts named (5,000-15,000 SEK for a few hours). For a student with rent due, this is powerful.
6. **Information asymmetry**: She never knows the organizer's real name, who the other men will be, or where the content ends up.

## Other Loopholes and Tactics

### 1. The "I'm Also a Performer" Shield
The organizer participates in the sexual acts himself and pays a friend to hold the camera. Now he's an "actor," not a "buyer." Who is the buyer? There isn't one. It's a "production."

### 2. The Consent Documentation Trick
Women sign a "model release" — a paper saying they consent to being filmed for adult content. This serves three purposes:
- Legal cover for the organizer ("she consented to perform")
- Leverage over the woman ("you signed a contract, we can publish this")
- Creates the appearance of a professional production

### 3. No Legal Definition of "Production"
Swedish law doesn't define what constitutes a legitimate pornographic production vs. a staged sex party. There are no requirements for:
- A registered production company
- A distribution agreement
- Crew (director, lighting, sound)
- Record-keeping of performer ages/IDs (unlike the US 2257 regulations)
- Any disclosure to Skatteverket about the nature of the business

This means **literally anyone with a phone camera can claim to be producing pornography**, and the burden of proof shifts to prosecutors to prove it was actually a sex purchase.

### 4. The International Production Dodge
"We're a [country X] production company filming in Sweden." Makes jurisdictional investigation harder. The "company" may be a shell registered in Estonia, UK, or Seychelles — costs €100 online.

### 5. Alcohol and Drugs as Lubricant
At the "party," alcohol is freely available. Sometimes drugs. This:
- Lowers inhibitions and resistance to acts that weren't discussed beforehand
- Compromises the woman's ability to consent (but since she signed a "release" sober, the organizer claims she consented)
- Makes her testimony unreliable if she later complains

### 6. The Shame Architecture
After the event:
- She was paid → "That makes me a sex worker"
- She signed a release → "I agreed to this"
- There are recordings → "If I complain, these could surface"
- She may not know the organizer's real name → "Who would I even report?"
- She may fear judgment → "People will think I'm a prostitute"

Each of these factors is **by design**. The structure is built so the woman self-silences.

### 7. The "Repeat Customer" Model
Men who attend these "parties" are never called clients or buyers. They're "actors" or "co-stars." They pay the organizer a fee (framed as a "production fee" or "membership") and in return get access to the event. The organizer profits from both sides: money from the men, and content he can sell or leverage.

## Why They Don't Get Caught

### Legal Barriers
- **Prosecution must prove it was NOT a pornographic production** — but there's no legal definition of what a "real" production looks like
- **No registration/licensing** for adult content production in Sweden means no records to audit
- **The women rarely report** — shame, self-blame, fear of recordings surfacing
- **Encrypted communications** mean police need advanced digital forensic warrants
- **No centralized database** — unlike the US's 2257 compliance system, Sweden has nothing tracking who produces what

### Practical Barriers
- Police exploitation units are **tiny and underfunded**
- These operations look different from traditional prostitution (no escort ads, no street presence)
- The organizer keeps a **very small circle** — 5-10 trusted men, 2-3 women per event
- Events are **infrequent** — once a month or less — making pattern detection hard
- **No victim complaint = no investigation** in practice

### The Culture Barrier
- Society still associates exploitation with visible, street-level prostitution
- "Porn production" is assumed to be legitimate by default
- The women are 18+, so child protection frameworks don't apply
- There's a cultural hesitation to question anything labeled "sex work" or "content creation"

## What Prevention Looks Like

### For Young Women (The Audience This Book Reaches)

**Red flags to recognize:**
1. 🔴 **No verifiable company** — no website, no org number, no named team
2. 🔴 **Contact only through encrypted/anonymous channels** — Telegram, Snap, burner numbers
3. 🔴 **Unusually high pay** for vague "modeling" or "content" work
4. 🔴 **You don't know who else will be there** until you arrive
5. 🔴 **Requests for photos/measurements before any meeting** (used to pre-screen and also create leverage)
6. 🔴 **The "shoot" is at a private location** — apartment, villa, hotel room — not a studio
7. 🔴 **Alcohol/drugs at a "professional" shoot** — real productions are sober workplaces
8. 🔴 **Pressure to decide fast** — "we're shooting this weekend, I need to know today"
9. 🔴 **The contract is vague or nonexistent** — or you can't take a copy home
10. 🔴 **You're told not to tell anyone** — secrecy is never a sign of legitimacy

**What a legitimate production looks like:**
- A registered company you can look up on allabolag.se
- Named producers/directors with public credits
- A contract reviewed before the day, ideally by your own representative
- Clear description of scenes before you agree
- Right to withdraw consent at any time during filming
- No alcohol or drugs on set
- Standard industry rates with proper payment (not cash in hand)
- Other crew present (lighting, sound, makeup — not just "friends with phones")

### For Policy / Systemic Change

1. **Require registration for adult content production** — like the US 2257 system. Any commercial production involving sexual acts must register, keep performer ID records, and be auditable.

2. **Define the legal boundary** — legislation should specify what constitutes a "pornographic production" vs. a sex purchase with a camera present. Criteria could include: registered company, distribution agreement, professional crew, proper performer contracts.

3. **Extend sexköpslagen explicitly** — make it illegal to pay for sexual access under the guise of production when no legitimate production infrastructure exists.

4. **Platform accountability** — require Telegram, Instagram, etc. to remove and report "casting call" content that lacks verifiable production credentials.

5. **Education** — this specific scenario should be part of sex education / digital literacy curricula for 16-18 year olds. Not as scare tactics, but as "here's how it actually works, here's what to check."

## Story Application: How This Scenario Could Work in the Book

### Character: **Klara, 21** — university student, tight on money

**Entry point:** She does some bikini modeling for Instagram. A DM arrives from "Nordic Visuals" — wants her for a "paid content shoot." First shoot is solo lingerie photos in a studio (rented for the day). Professional. She gets 5,000 SEK cash. Feels good.

**Escalation:** Second shoot is with another girl. Implied nudity. 8,000 SEK. Third shoot: "we're doing couples content" — there's a man. She hesitates. "You've already done two shoots, this is just the next level. Everyone else does it."

**The party:** Fourth time, it's a villa. Multiple men. Cameras everywhere ("it's a multi-scene production day"). Alcohol. The line between "shoot" and "party" has vanished. She's paid 15,000 SEK.

**The trap:** She realizes what it was. But:
- She doesn't know the organizer's real name (only "Marcus" on Telegram)
- She signed a release form
- She was paid — does that make her a prostitute?
- There are recordings she can't control
- She blames herself

**The blindspot:** Her friends think she's doing modeling work. Her parents have no idea. The organizer has committed no crime anyone can prove, because the cameras were rolling and the money was labeled "performance compensation."
