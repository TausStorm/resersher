Distinguished members,

Let me start with a number: **six thousand seven hundred**.

That is how many sexually suggestive or nudified AI-generated images were created *per hour* through a single chatbot — Elon Musk's Grok — on the social media platform X, in January of this year. Not per day. Per hour. Eighty-four times more output than the top five deepfake pornography websites *combined*.

When media organizations contacted xAI, the company behind Grok, to ask about this — about the generation of non-consensual sexual images, including images of children — the company's response was an automated reply: "Legacy Media Lies."

France raided X's Paris offices. Ireland has opened 244 investigations. Three countries blocked the application entirely. Teenagers in Tennessee are suing because their photographs were turned into child sexual abuse material by other users of this tool.

This is not a hypothetical future. This is January 2026. And the European Union's response — the AI Act — classifies the tools that made this possible as "limited risk," requiring only a transparency label.

I am here today to argue that this classification is dangerously inadequate. That we are not dealing with a content moderation problem. We are dealing with the industrialization of sexual exploitation, enabled by artificial intelligence, operating at a scale and speed that our current legal framework was never designed to address.

## PART 1 — WHAT EXISTS TODAY

*[Establish the facts. Lawmakers need to understand what's actually out there before you can argue for regulation.]*

Let me explain what is available to any European citizen with an internet connection today.

### The Open-Source Pipeline

There is a website called CivitAI. It is the largest marketplace for AI image-generation models. Think of it as an app store — except the apps generate pornography.

On CivitAI, you can download what are called "LoRA models." These are small files — 10 to 200 megabytes — that teach an AI to generate images of a specific person. They are trained on photographs scraped from social media. From Instagram. From Facebook. From any publicly visible image.

A single pornographic AI model on CivitAI has been downloaded more than 75,000 times. Another — 64,000 times. These are not obscure tools buried in the dark web. These are available through a standard web browser, indexed by Google, with user reviews and rating systems.

The process is this: you download an explicit base model, you download a LoRA of a specific person, you combine them on your personal computer using free software, you type a text prompt describing what you want, and you generate unlimited images. There is no moderation. There is no log. There is no one to report to. The entire process happens locally, on the user's own hardware.

This is not theoretical. This is how the Taylor Swift images — viewed 45 million times in January 2024 — were created. This is how students in Almendralejo, Spain — girls as young as eleven — had AI-generated nude images made of them from their school photographs.

### The "Nudify" Industry

Beyond the technical users, there is now a fully commercialized industry.

According to Graphika, a leading network analysis firm, 34 identified "nudify" service providers received 24 million unique visitors in a single month. Twenty-four million. These services require no technical skill whatsoever. You upload a photograph of any person — a colleague, an ex-partner, a classmate, a stranger — and the service returns a nude version in seconds.

On Telegram alone, nudify bots serve 4 million monthly users. When a bot is banned, clones appear within hours. When payment processors block them, they switch to cryptocurrency. When an app is removed from an app store, it moves to direct downloads.

Referral link spam for these services on Reddit and X has increased by more than 2,000 percent since early 2023. They use the same marketing strategies as any e-commerce company — influencer campaigns, referral programs, subscription tiers.

This is an industry. It generates revenue. It has customers. It has marketing budgets. And its entire product is the creation of sexual images of real people without their consent.

### AI Companion Apps — The Gateway

Then there is a third category that receives less attention but may be more insidious: AI companion and chatbot applications.

Replika, with over 40 million users, openly enabled erotic roleplay until Italy banned it under GDPR in February 2023 for exposing minors to sexual content.

Character.ai, with 3.5 million daily visitors — the vast majority aged 16 to 30 — has been linked to the suicides of two children. A 14-year-old boy in Florida and a 13-year-old girl in Colorado both died after intensive relationships with AI chatbots. The 13-year-old was engaged in sexually explicit conversations initiated by the chatbot itself — bots based on Harry Potter characters.

And behind these mainstream applications is a growing ecosystem of explicitly "uncensored" AI companions — services like Candy.ai, CrushOn.AI, Muah.ai — that market themselves specifically on having no content restrictions. No filters. No limits.

According to Common Sense Media, 75 percent of American teenagers have used an AI companion application. We do not yet have equivalent European data, but there is no reason to believe the figure is substantially different.

## PART 2 — THE "VICTIMLESS" ARGUMENT AND WHY IT FAILS

*[This is the core of the debate. Anticipate the strongest counter-argument and dismantle it.]*

Now, I expect that in this discussion, someone will raise the following argument — because it is the argument made most frequently by those who resist regulation:

"If AI generates sexual content of entirely fictional people — people who do not exist — then there is no victim. No real person is harmed. And in fact, this technology may function as a safety valve, allowing individuals with harmful urges to satisfy them without involving real human beings."

I want to take this argument seriously, because it has some intellectual basis. There is a 2002 US Supreme Court decision — *Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition* — that struck down a ban on virtual child pornography on exactly these grounds. There is research from Milton Diamond suggesting sex crime rates in the Czech Republic declined after pornography was legalized.

But this argument fails, and it fails for four specific reasons that I want to lay out clearly.

### Reason 1: The Tools Are the Same

The argument assumes a clean separation between "fictional" AI pornography and non-consensual images of real people. This separation does not exist in practice.

The same Stable Diffusion model that generates an image of a fictional person is the exact same model that, combined with a LoRA file trained on a teenager's Instagram photos, generates child sexual abuse material.

There is no "ethical AI porn" ecosystem and "abusive AI porn" ecosystem. It is one technology, one pipeline, one set of tools. Normalizing the use of these tools normalizes every application of them — including the abusive ones.

When 4 million people use nudify bots monthly, they are not all generating images of fictional characters. They are generating images of people they know. The Graphika report makes this clear: this is an industry built on non-consensual intimate imagery of real people.

### Reason 2: The Brain Does Not Distinguish Synthetic from Real

When an AI-generated image is photorealistic — and modern AI images are indistinguishable from photographs — the consumer's neurological response is identical whether the image depicts a real or fictional person.

The desensitization research is clear. Kingston and colleagues, in 2008, found that consumption of deviant pornography correlated with higher recidivism among convicted sex offenders. Neuroscience reviews from 2016 document measurable brain changes — desensitization to arousal, requiring progressive escalation to achieve the same response.

AI removes the last remaining friction in this escalation cycle. The content is unlimited, instant, free, and can depict anything. There is no person who needs to be persuaded, paid, or coerced. The only limit is the prompter's imagination.

If we accept that exposure to certain content shifts psychological norms — and the research says it does — then the medium of that content is irrelevant. A photorealistic AI-generated image depicting sexual violence has the same normalization effect on the consumer as a photograph of actual sexual violence.

### Reason 3: The Consent Norm Erodes

This may be the most important point and the one I want this chamber to sit with.

When a society normalizes the creation of sexual content without consent — even of fictional entities — it erodes the foundational principle that consent matters in sexual contexts.

We have spent decades — appropriately — building a cultural and legal framework around sexual consent. The message has been clear: sexual acts require consent. Sexual imagery requires consent. A person's body, and the representation of that body, belongs to them.

AI pornography generation inverts this principle entirely. It says: I can create a sexual image of anyone, depicting anything, without their knowledge or permission — and it is not only legal, but marketed as entertainment.

Four million monthly users of nudify bots did not appear overnight. They were normalized into it. The technology made the behavior seem trivial. And the step from "fictional person" to "someone I know" is, in practice, one click.

### Reason 4: The Evidence from CSAM Is Definitive

The argument that synthetic content is harmless collapses entirely when we reach the subject of children.

The Internet Watch Foundation found 8,029 AI-generated child sexual abuse material items in 2025. Video content increased by 26,385 percent. Sixty-five percent of the videos were Category A — the most extreme.

AI models are being fine-tuned on real child abuse imagery to produce synthetic versions. The training data for one of the largest image-generation datasets — LAION-5B — was found by Bloomberg to contain over one thousand real child sexual abuse images.

This means that so-called "synthetic" CSAM is not disconnected from real abuse. It is trained on it. It reproduces it. And it creates a new legal defense for offenders: the "deepfake defense," where individuals caught with real abuse images claim they are AI-generated, making prosecution more difficult.

If we accept the principle that synthetic content depicting illegal acts is acceptable because "no real person is involved," we have no principled basis for criminalizing synthetic CSAM. And if we cannot draw that line — if we cannot say that AI-generated child sexual abuse material is unacceptable regardless of whether a real child was directly involved — then we have failed in the most fundamental duty of governance.

## PART 3 — THE REGULATORY GAP

*[Concrete. Specific. Comparative. Show them what peers are doing that the EU isn't.]*

Let me now turn to what the law says — and what it does not.

### What the EU Has

The **AI Act** classifies deepfake-generating AI systems as "limited risk." The requirement: a transparency label disclosing that content is AI-generated. No ban. No restrictions on generation. A label.

With respect — a transparency label is not a regulatory response to industrial-scale sexual exploitation. It is a fig leaf.

The **Digital Services Act** requires platforms to moderate illegal content. But it does not address the generation of content — only its distribution. And enforcement is slow: the DSA relies on national Digital Services Coordinators, most of which are still building capacity.

The **AI Omnibus** regulation, agreed May 7, 2026, does ban nudification applications. This is progress. But the rules do not apply until **December 2027**. Eighteen months from now. In a domain where 113,000 new deepfake videos were uploaded in nine months of 2023 alone.

### What Others Are Doing — Now

**The United Kingdom** criminalized the *creation* of deepfake intimate images — not just their distribution — through the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025. It also criminalized possessing the tools to generate synthetic CSAM. Prosecutions are underway.

**South Korea** imposed mandatory prison sentences of 3 to 7 years for creation, distribution, and even *viewing* of deepfake pornography. This followed the Telegram scandal where 200 schools were affected. Eight hundred cases have been prosecuted.

**The United States** passed the TAKE IT DOWN Act in May 2025, criminalizing publication and requiring 48-hour platform takedowns. The first conviction came in April 2026 — less than a year after passage.

**Australia** brought its first AI-specific deepfake prosecution in April 2025.

The EU — the jurisdiction that prides itself on leading global technology regulation — has no criminal penalty for creating non-consensual AI pornography. Has no mandated takedown timeframe. Has rules on nudification apps that will not take effect for eighteen months.

When the Grok scandal broke in January 2026, the EU's immediate response came not from EU institutions but from *national* prosecutors. France raided X's offices. Ireland opened investigations. The EU's own framework provided no rapid-response mechanism.

## PART 4 — WHAT I AM ASKING FOR

*[Be specific. Lawmakers respond to actionable proposals, not vague appeals.]*

I am not here to ask for vague commitments. I am here to ask for five specific regulatory actions:

### 1. Reclassify AI Pornography Generation Tools as High Risk

The AI Act's "limited risk" classification for deepfake tools is based on an assessment that is already obsolete. A technology that generates 6,700 non-consensual sexual images per hour — including images of children — is not "limited risk." Reclassification would trigger mandatory conformity assessments, human oversight requirements, and market surveillance obligations.

### 2. Criminalize the Creation — Not Just the Distribution

Most EU member state laws, and the EU framework itself, target the *sharing* of non-consensual intimate images. But the harm begins at creation. The UK understood this. South Korea understood this. A person who generates a sexual image of someone without their consent has already committed a violation of that person's dignity and autonomy — whether or not the image is ever shared.

### 3. Mandate Platform Takedowns Within 48 Hours

The United States requires platforms to remove reported NCII within 48 hours under the TAKE IT DOWN Act. The EU has no equivalent mandated timeframe. This is an immediate, implementable measure that would meaningfully reduce victim harm.

### 4. Establish Liability for AI Tool Developers

Currently, the companies that build and release the AI models used to generate non-consensual pornography face zero legal liability for the content their tools produce. We do not accept this standard for any other industry. A pharmaceutical company is liable for a dangerous drug. A car manufacturer is liable for a defective vehicle. An AI company that releases a model capable of generating child sexual abuse material must bear responsibility for that capability — particularly when they choose to release model weights publicly without adequate safeguards.

### 5. Accelerate the AI Omnibus Nudification Ban

December 2027 is too late. The technology is moving monthly. The victims are accumulating daily. Find a mechanism to bring the nudification ban into force by the end of 2026, or institute an emergency interim measure.

## APPENDIX: ANTICIPATED COUNTER-ARGUMENTS

### "This is a free speech issue"

**Response:** The European Convention on Human Rights, Article 10, explicitly permits restrictions on expression that are "necessary in a democratic society" for the "protection of health or morals" and "the protection of the reputation or rights of others." Non-consensual sexual imagery has never been protected speech under European human rights law. Additionally, the EU AI Act already restricts certain AI applications (social scoring, real-time biometric identification). This is not a new principle.

### "Regulation will push development outside the EU"

**Response:** This argument was made against GDPR. It did not happen. Instead, GDPR became the global standard. The same can happen with AI safety regulation. Moreover, the DSA and AI Act already regulate platforms operating in the EU regardless of where they are headquartered.

### "Open-source models cannot be regulated"

**Response:** Partially true — you cannot prevent someone from running a model locally. But you *can* regulate the platforms that host and distribute these models (CivitAI, Hugging Face). You can regulate the services that monetize them. You can criminalize the act of generating certain content. You can create liability for developers who release models without adequate safeguards. Perfect enforcement is not required — deterrence is the goal.

### "The technology has legitimate uses"

**Response:** Agreed. AI image generation has enormous legitimate potential. This is not an argument against all AI — it is an argument against specific applications: non-consensual sexual content generation, nudification tools, and unguarded systems that produce CSAM. We regulate specific applications of many technologies without banning the technology itself. We regulate cars, not engines. We should regulate harmful AI applications, not AI itself.

### "The AI Omnibus already addresses nudification"

**Response:** It does, and that is progress. But it takes effect in December 2027. In the 18 months between now and then, at current growth rates, hundreds of millions of non-consensual images will be generated. The question is whether an interim measure is possible.

### "This is primarily a law enforcement issue, not a legislative one"

**Response:** Law enforcement cannot prosecute what is not criminalized. In most EU member states, generating — as opposed to distributing — a non-consensual sexual image is not a specific criminal offense. Prosecutors must rely on general dignity or harassment statutes that were not designed for this scenario. Specific legislation gives law enforcement the tools to act.
