# EU Regulatory Landscape: AI-Generated Pornographic Content

*Research brief for EU debate presentation — May 2026*

---

## 1. EU AI Act Provisions on Sexual Content

### What the AI Act says (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689)

The original AI Act (entered into force 1 Aug 2024) does **not** specifically classify NSFW AI tools as high-risk. Sexual content generation tools fall under **"minimal or no risk"** by default unless they involve:

- **Transparency obligations (Article 50)**: AI-generated content — including deepfakes — must be labelled as AI-generated. This applies to images, audio, video. Effective **August 2026**.
- **General-purpose AI (GPAI) rules** (effective Aug 2025): Providers of GPAI models must comply with transparency and copyright rules, but there is no specific prohibition on generating sexual content.
- **No explicit ban on NSFW tools**: The original AI Act did not prohibit AI nudification tools, deepfake porn generators, or AI-generated sexual content of adults.

### The AI Omnibus — May 2026 breakthrough

On **7 May 2026**, EU Parliament and Council reached political agreement on the "Digital Omnibus on AI" which adds a critical new provision:

> **Prohibition of AI systems that generate non-consensual sexually explicit and intimate content or child sexual abuse material (CSAM), such as AI "nudification" apps.**

This is now explicitly listed as an **unacceptable risk** practice under the AI Act. This was added to the original eight prohibited practices.

**Key criticisms of the original AI Act (pre-omnibus):**
- No specific mention of deepfake porn or nudification tools
- Sexual content generation fell into a regulatory blind spot — not high-risk, not prohibited
- Transparency rules (labelling deepfakes) don't take effect until Aug 2026 — too late
- No requirement for AI providers to implement consent verification
- No mechanism for victims to request removal of AI-generated sexual content

**Key criticism of the omnibus fix:**
- Only covers "non-consensual" — consensual adult AI porn remains unregulated
- No retroactive application to content already generated
- Enforcement details still being worked out
- Implementation timeline: Rules for high-risk areas apply from **2 December 2027**; product-integrated systems from **2 August 2028**

## 2. Digital Services Act (DSA) — Regulation (EU) 2022/2065

### Coverage of AI-generated porn

The DSA does **not** specifically address AI-generated pornography. It applies to **all illegal content** on digital platforms, with a tiered system based on platform size:

- **Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs)**: 45M+ monthly EU users — subject to strictest obligations. X (Twitter) is a designated VLOP. Pornhub, XVideos, XNXX, and Stripchat are also designated VLOPs.
- **Illegal content removal**: Platforms must act expeditiously once notified of illegal content, including non-consensual intimate imagery where national law criminalizes it.
- **Algorithmic transparency**: VLOPs must conduct systemic risk assessments, including risks from "dissemination of illegal content" and negative effects on fundamental rights.
- **Article 40**: Requires VLOPs to grant data access to researchers to detect systemic risks.

### Enforcement actions relevant to deepfake porn

- **Dec 2025**: Commission fined X €120 million for DSA violations (deceptive blue checkmarks, ad transparency failures, researcher data access barriers). Not directly about sexual content, but establishes enforcement precedent.
- **Jan 2026 (Grok scandal)**: Ireland's Coimisiún na Meán consulted with European Commission about Grok generating sexualized images. Ireland's DPC opened investigation under GDPR Articles 5, 6, 25, 35. Ofcom (UK) also contacted X.
- **Feb 2026**: Paris prosecutors' cybercrime team and Europol **raided X's Paris offices** over sexual deepfakes and Holocaust denial. Elon Musk and Linda Yaccarino were summoned to hearing on April 20 — Musk did not appear.

### DSA gaps for AI-generated sexual content

- **No proactive monitoring obligation**: DSA does not require platforms to actively scan for AI-generated sexual content
- **Notice-and-action only**: Platforms must respond to reports but need not prevent generation
- **AI tool providers not covered**: DSA covers hosting/distribution, not the AI tool that creates the content
- **Speed**: 48-hour takedown not mandated specifically (unlike US TAKE IT DOWN Act)
- **Cross-border enforcement challenges**: X Corp. headquartered in US, DSA coordination across 27 member states slow

## 3. Member State National Laws

### France
- **Jan 2, 2026**: French government ministers reported Grok's sexually explicit AI content to prosecutors, calling it "manifestly illegal" (*"manifestement illégal"*)
- **Feb 3, 2026**: Paris cybercrime unit raided X's Paris offices. Investigation covers algorithmic abuse, fraudulent data extraction, Holocaust denial, and sexual deepfakes
- **Apr 20, 2026**: Musk summoned to hearing, did not appear. Paris prosecutors stated his absence is "not an obstacle to continuing the investigation"
- France's existing **Loi Avia** (2020) on online hate speech was partially struck down by Constitutional Council but elements remain. The DSA has largely superseded national online content law
- France has strong criminal provisions on voyeurism and non-consensual intimate imagery under the Penal Code

### Germany
- Justice Minister **Stefanie Hubig** announced a new law against deepfake pornography is "ready and will be presented in a very short time"
- Proposal aims to extend police authority to search suspects' devices and improve law enforcement identification of offenders
- Germany's **NetzDG** (Network Enforcement Act, 2017) was one of the first platform content laws globally — now largely superseded by DSA
- Existing criminal code (StGB §184b) criminalizes distribution of child pornography including synthetic/AI-generated images

### Spain
- Spain transposed EU Directive 2011/93/EU on combating sexual abuse of children, which covers "visual depictions" including computer-generated images
- No specific national law on AI deepfake pornography for adults yet

### Italy
- **"Revenge porn" law** (2019, Art. 612-ter Codice Penale): Criminalizes non-consensual sharing of intimate images, applicable to deepfakes
- No specific AI-generated content legislation yet

### Netherlands
- Strong existing laws on revenge porn and CSAM
- Netherlands hosted the 2025 NATO summit — digital safety increasingly on political agenda
- No specific AI deepfake porn legislation

### Ireland
- **Jan 2026**: Coimisiún na Meán consulted European Commission on Grok deepfakes
- **Jan 13**: Taoiseach Micheál Martin announced discussions with Attorney General over Grok
- **Jan 14**: Garda Síochána announced **200+ investigations** into child sex abuse images generated by Grok (grew to 244 by March)
- Criminal prosecution possible under: Harassment, Harmful Communications and Related Offences Act 2020; Non-Fatal Offences Against the Person Act 1997; Child Trafficking and Pornography Act 1998
- **Feb 2026**: Data Protection Commissioner opened investigation under GDPR

### Poland
- **Jan 2026**: President Karol Nawrocki vetoed a bill designating a DSA enforcement authority, calling it a threat to free speech — comparing it to Orwell's Ministry of Truth

### Missing: No strong individual MEP quotes found on AI porn specifically

## 5. EU Child Protection Frameworks and Synthetic CSAM

### EU Directive 2011/93/EU on combating sexual abuse of children
- **Article 5(6)**: Criminalizes production of child pornography including images that "appear to be" a child engaged in sexual conduct — **explicitly covers AI-generated/synthetic CSAM**
- **Article 2(c)** defines child pornography to include "realistic images of a child engaged in sexually explicit conduct" regardless of whether a real child was involved
- Requires member states to criminalize production, distribution, and possession
- **Penalty framework**: At least 2-3 years imprisonment for production/distribution

### Lanzarote Convention (Council of Europe Convention on Protection of Children against Sexual Exploitation, 2007)
- **Article 20(2)**: Parties may criminalize production of child pornography involving "realistic images of a non-existent child"
- **Signatory compliance**: Most EU member states have signed and ratified, but implementation of the "non-existent child" provision varies — some countries made reservations
- **Gap**: Originally drafted before AI image generation existed. "Realistic images" was meant for CGI/photo manipulation. AI-generated content tests the boundaries of these definitions

### Proposed EU Child Sexual Abuse Regulation ("Chat Control")
- Commission proposed regulation requiring platforms to detect, report, and remove CSAM — including new material and grooming
- **Controversial**: Criticized for potentially undermining end-to-end encryption
- Would cover AI-generated CSAM
- Still under negotiation as of May 2026 — one of the most contested EU legislative proposals

### The AI Omnibus (May 2026) CSAM provision
- Explicitly prohibits AI systems that generate CSAM
- Added to the AI Act's "unacceptable risk" category
- Stronger than the transparency-only approach of the original AI Act

### Remaining gaps in child protection
- **Student-on-student AI CSAM**: 404 Media (May 2025) reported that no one knows how to handle AI deepfake porn created by minors of other minors — a major enforcement blind spot
- **Detection**: No mandated detection technology for AI-generated CSAM vs. real CSAM
- **Cross-platform coordination**: No EU mechanism for hash-sharing of AI-generated CSAM across platforms
- **Scale**: By Oct 2024, "nudify" deepfake bots on Telegram had up to **4 million monthly users**

- **UK criminalized creation** — EU only prohibits AI systems (provider-side), not the act of creating a specific image
- **South Korea criminalizes possession/viewing** — EU has no such provision
- **US mandates 48-hour takedown** — EU DSA has no specific timeframe for AI-generated sexual content
- **EU enforcement is slowest**: UK acted within days of Grok scandal (Jan 12 — Ofcom investigation, Jan 12 — criminalization enabled). EU AI Omnibus rules won't apply until Dec 2027 at earliest.

## 7. Proposed or Pending EU Legislation

### Confirmed / In progress:

1. **AI Act Omnibus ("Digital Omnibus on AI")** — Political agreement May 7, 2026
   - Bans nudification apps and non-consensual AI sexual content
   - Implementation: Dec 2027 (standalone) / Aug 2028 (product-integrated)
   - Formal adoption expected H2 2026

2. **EU Child Sexual Abuse Regulation ("Chat Control")** — Still under negotiation
   - Would mandate detection and reporting of CSAM including AI-generated
   - Encryption concerns remain unresolved
   - No agreement timeline clear

3. **Commission guidelines on transparency obligations (Article 50)** — Consultation opened May 8, 2026
   - Code of Practice on marking and labelling AI-generated content
   - Will clarify deepfake labelling requirements
   - Expected Q2 2026

4. **Commission guidelines on high-risk AI classification** — Consultation opened May 19, 2026
   - May clarify whether certain sexual content tools could be classified as high-risk

5. **Review of prohibited practices and high-risk AI** — Report published May 22, 2026
   - Post-implementation review of AI Act prohibitions

### Not yet proposed but needed:

- **Dedicated EU directive on non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII)** — No equivalent to US TAKE IT DOWN Act
- **EU-wide criminal law on deepfake porn creation** — Currently only national criminal laws apply
- **Harmonized victim remedy framework** — No EU-level right for victims to compel removal across all 27 states
- **AI safety-by-design requirements for generative AI** — Requiring consent verification, age verification, and content filtering at model level

## 8. Expert Recommendations for EU Policy

### Based on research findings, the following recommendations have emerged:

**From civil society and NGOs:**
- **Get Grok Gone campaign** (Jan 2026): Demanded Apple and Google remove X/Grok apps from stores for enabling NCII and CSAM
- **AI Forensics** (Paris-based nonprofit): Analysis showed ~10% of recovered Grok content on standalone site/app showed "photorealistic people, very young, doing sexual activities" — recommends app store accountability
- Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch have called for restrictions on surveillance-based advertising and stronger fundamental rights protections in AI regulation

**From enforcement experience:**
- Irish Garda: 244 investigations into Grok-generated CSAM as of March 2026 — demonstrates existing law can be used but scale overwhelms enforcement capacity
- French prosecutors: Raided X offices, summoned Musk — demonstrates DSA enforcement tools exist but are slow
- California AG Rob Bonta (Jan 14, 2026): Launched state investigation into xAI, stating "the avalanche of reports detailing the non-consensual, sexually explicit material that xAI has produced and posted online in recent weeks is shocking"

**Academic/legal recommendations:**
- Clare McGlynn & Rüya Tuna Toparlak (2025, Journal of Law and Society): Proposed criminalizing creation of deepfake porn as a "new voyeurism" — arguing the harm occurs at the moment of generation, not just distribution
- Hany Farid (UC Berkeley, digital image analysis): Emphasized distinction between deepfakes (non-consensual alteration of real people) and purely synthetic AI content — both need regulation but different approaches
- Product safety framing: Legal scholars argue AI-generated sexual imagery should be treated as product safety/personal injury harm, not just privacy violation — liability should focus on system design and safety safeguards

**Policy gap analysis — what EU lawmakers should prioritize:**

1. **Close the creation gap**: EU bans the AI system but doesn't criminalize the act of creating specific deepfake images of identifiable people. UK does. South Korea does. The EU should too.

2. **Speed up enforcement timeline**: AI Omnibus rules apply from Dec 2027 — 18+ months from now. The Grok scandal is happening NOW. Interim enforcement through DSA should be explicitly empowered for AI-generated NCII.

3. **Mandate platform takedown timeframes**: US requires 48 hours. EU has no specific timeframe. Victims need a guaranteed removal timeline.

4. **Require safety-by-design for generative AI**: AI models should be required to implement consent verification, age verification for subjects, and robust content filtering — at the model level, not just the platform level.

5. **Harmonize criminal law across member states**: Currently, whether creating a deepfake nude is criminal depends on which country you're in. An EU directive should establish minimum criminal penalties.

6. **Address the "student-on-student" gap**: Minors creating AI CSAM of other minors is an enforcement blind spot across all jurisdictions. Educational and preventive frameworks needed alongside criminal law.

7. **Fund detection capabilities**: No EU-level investment in AI-generated CSAM detection technology. Hash-sharing systems like PhotoDNA need AI-generation equivalents.

8. **Hold app stores accountable**: Apple and Google profit from hosting apps that can be used to generate NCII. DSA obligations should extend to distribution infrastructure.

|---|---|---|

| National criminal laws (France, Germany, Ireland, Italy) | Review of AI Act prohibitions (May 2026) | Victim remedy framework (right to removal) |
| Lanzarote Convention — covers "realistic images" of children | | EU-level detection technology investment |
| | | App store accountability framework |

**Bottom line for EU lawmakers**: The EU has built the world's most comprehensive AI regulatory framework (AI Act + DSA + GDPR), but it has a critical enforcement gap on AI-generated sexual content. The AI Omnibus fixes the legislative prohibition, but rules don't apply until late 2027. Other jurisdictions — UK, South Korea, US — are already prosecuting. The Grok scandal of Jan 2026 exposed that enforcement depends on national prosecutors (France, Ireland) rather than EU-level mechanisms, and that platforms can generate thousands of harmful images per hour while regulatory response takes months. The urgency gap between harm velocity and regulatory speed is the central challenge.
