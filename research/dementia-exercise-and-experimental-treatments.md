**Research compiled April 2026 | Focus: Beyond standard medical care**

## PART 1: EXERCISE AND DEMENTIA

### 1.1 Which types of exercise have the strongest evidence?

**Ranked by strength of evidence:**

1. **Aerobic exercise** -- Strongest evidence overall. Meta-analyses (2023-2025) consistently show aerobic exercise improves cognitive function across all domains. Walking, cycling, swimming, and dancing at moderate-to-vigorous intensity show the most robust effects. A 2024 Lancet Commission update reaffirmed physical inactivity as one of the top modifiable risk factors for dementia, attributing ~2% of global cases to it.

2. **Combined aerobic + resistance training** -- Emerging evidence (2023-2024) suggests the combination outperforms either alone. The SYNERGIC trial (Canada, 2023) showed multi-domain exercise interventions (aerobic + resistance + balance + cognitive) produced larger effects than single-modality approaches.

3. **Resistance/strength training** -- Growing evidence, especially for executive function. The SMART trial (Australia) showed high-intensity progressive resistance training (2x/week, 6 months) improved global cognition in MCI patients, with benefits lasting 12 months after cessation. Mechanisms differ from aerobic: increases IGF-1 rather than BDNF primarily.

4. **Dance** -- Uniquely combines physical, cognitive, social, and emotional engagement. A 2023 systematic review found dance-based interventions superior to walking for executive function in older adults. The dual-task nature makes it particularly powerful.

5. **HIIT (High-Intensity Interval Training)** -- Limited but promising evidence. A 2024 study showed HIIT produces greater acute BDNF increases than moderate continuous training. However, adherence concerns in older/cognitively impaired populations.

6. **Yoga/Tai Chi** -- Moderate evidence. Benefits appear primarily through stress reduction (cortisol lowering), improved sleep quality, and inflammation reduction rather than direct neuroplasticity mechanisms. Better studied for subjective cognitive complaints than diagnosed dementia.

**Key finding:** The type matters less than consistency and adequate intensity. Any exercise that raises heart rate to 60-80% of max HR and is sustained over months produces measurable brain changes.

### 1.2 BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor)

**What it is:** BDNF is a protein that acts as "fertilizer" for brain cells. It supports survival of existing neurons, encourages growth of new neurons (neurogenesis), and strengthens synaptic connections (long-term potentiation -- the cellular basis of learning and memory).

**How exercise increases BDNF:**

- Acute exercise increases peripheral BDNF levels by 20-30% (meta-analysis, Dinoff et al.)
- The increase is intensity-dependent: vigorous exercise > moderate > light
- Aerobic exercise produces the most reliable BDNF increases
- The BDNF increase is transient (returns to baseline within hours), but regular exercise raises baseline BDNF levels over weeks/months
- Mechanism: muscle contraction releases irisin (via PGC-1alpha/FNDC5 pathway), which crosses the blood-brain barrier and stimulates hippocampal BDNF expression
- Exercise also increases BDNF through lactate signaling: blood lactate from exercising muscles enters the brain and activates BDNF gene expression via SIRT1/PGC-1alpha

**What BDNF does for the brain:**

- Stimulates hippocampal neurogenesis (new neurons in the memory center)
- Enhances synaptic plasticity and long-term potentiation
- Protects against neuronal death from amyloid-beta toxicity
- Supports cholinergic neuron function (the system most damaged in AD)
- Reduces neuroinflammation
- Promotes cerebrovascular health

**Critical finding (2023-2024):** BDNF levels are significantly lower in Alzheimer's patients compared to healthy controls, and the decline correlates with disease progression. This has led researchers to describe AD partly as a "BDNF deficiency syndrome," making exercise-induced BDNF one of the most compelling intervention targets.

**The Val66Met polymorphism:** About 20-30% of the population carries the BDNF Val66Met variant, which reduces activity-dependent BDNF secretion. These individuals may need higher exercise doses to achieve the same neuroprotective benefit. This is relevant for personalized exercise prescriptions.

### 1.3 Optimal Protocol: How much, how often, what intensity?

**Current best-evidence protocol (synthesized from multiple 2023-2025 meta-analyses):**

| Parameter | Recommendation | Evidence Level |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| **Frequency** | 3-5 days/week (minimum 3) | Strong |
| **Duration** | 30-45 min per session | Strong |
| **Weekly total** | 150-300 min moderate OR 75-150 min vigorous | Strong |
| **Intensity** | Moderate-to-vigorous (60-80% max HR) | Strong |
| **Type** | Primarily aerobic, add 2x/week resistance | Moderate-Strong |
| **Duration of program** | Minimum 12 weeks for measurable brain changes; 6+ months for lasting benefit | Strong |

**Dose-response insights (2024):**

- A 2024 prospective study (~79,000 participants, UK Biobank) found a dose-response relationship: every additional 10 min/day of moderate-vigorous physical activity reduced dementia risk by ~8%
- Benefits begin at even low levels of activity (vs. sedentary), but peak benefit appears around 150-300 min/week of moderate activity
- There is NO upper limit where exercise becomes harmful to the brain (unlike some cardiovascular endpoints)
- Higher cardiorespiratory fitness (VO2max) is independently associated with lower dementia risk, suggesting the cardiovascular benefits are a key mediator

**For prevention (cognitively healthy):**
- 150+ min/week moderate aerobic exercise
- 2x/week resistance training
- Regular cognitive engagement during exercise (dual-task)

**For MCI (mild cognitive impairment):**
- Same as above, but structured/supervised programs show better outcomes
- Nordic walking has shown particular promise in Nordic studies

**For diagnosed dementia:**
- See section 1.4 below

### 1.4 Exercise in Existing Dementia: Can it slow progression?

**The evidence is more mixed for diagnosed dementia than for prevention, but still positive:**

**What works:**

- **Walking programs (supervised):** The FINALEX RCT (Finland, 2013) and subsequent studies showed supervised walking 3x/week slowed functional decline over 12 months in moderate AD. More recent evidence (2023) reaffirms this for mild-moderate dementia.
- **Multi-component exercise:** A 2024 Cochrane review update found that exercise programs combining aerobic, strength, and balance training improved activities of daily living (ADLs) and reduced behavioral and psychological symptoms of dementia (BPSD) -- agitation, depression, sleep disturbance.
- **High-intensity functional exercise (HIFE):** The Swedish Umea HIFE program showed that high-intensity functional exercises (sit-to-stand, stepping, weight-bearing) improved balance and reduced falls in nursing home residents with dementia.

**What the evidence says about slowing cognitive decline specifically:**

- Effects on cognition in diagnosed dementia are modest and variable
- Exercise more reliably improves: mood, sleep, behavior, functional capacity, caregiver burden
- The DAPA trial (UK, 2018) -- a large RCT of moderate-high intensity aerobic+strength exercise in mild-moderate AD -- did NOT show cognitive benefit at 12 months, which tempered enthusiasm
- However, the ADEX trial (Denmark, 2024 follow-up) showed that moderate-high intensity aerobic exercise reduced neuropsychiatric symptoms and improved cardiovascular fitness in AD patients
- Best evidence for slowing cognitive decline is in MCI, not established dementia

**Practical recommendations for someone with dementia:**

1. Focus on what they can do, not what they can't
2. Walking is the most accessible and evidence-based option
3. Supervised/group programs improve adherence
4. Music-based movement/dance has strong evidence for engagement and mood
5. Even chair-based exercises show benefits for advanced dementia
6. Prioritize fall prevention alongside aerobic benefit
7. Consistency matters more than intensity

### 1.5 Dual-Task Training

**What it is:** Simultaneously combining physical exercise with cognitive tasks -- e.g., walking while doing mental arithmetic, stepping exercises while memorizing words, cycling while playing cognitive games on a screen.

**Why it matters:** Real-life function depends on the ability to manage physical and cognitive demands simultaneously (walking while talking, navigating while remembering directions). This "dual-task" ability deteriorates early in dementia and is a strong predictor of falls.

**Evidence (2022-2025):**

- A 2023 meta-analysis (Wollesen et al.) of 25 RCTs found dual-task training superior to single-task exercise for executive function and divided attention in older adults with MCI
- The GEP-NEC study (Germany, 2024) showed combined cognitive-motor training on interactive exercise equipment improved global cognition vs. exercise alone in MCI
- The SYNERGIC trial (2023) confirmed multi-domain interventions (physical+cognitive) outperform single-domain approaches
- Exergaming (exercise video games like dance pads, VR-based exercise) is an accessible form of dual-task training showing promising results, particularly in care settings

**Specific dual-task examples with evidence:**

- Walking + verbal fluency tasks
- Stationary cycling + tablet-based cognitive games
- Dance (inherently dual-task: movement patterns + music + social)
- Tai Chi (movement sequences require cognitive engagement)
- Circuit training with cognitive challenges at each station

**Swedish/Nordic context:** Several Swedish universities (Karolinska, Umea, Gothenburg) are actively researching dual-task interventions in dementia care settings. The Swedish dementia care model ("personcentrerad vard") increasingly integrates physical activity programs.

## PART 2: EXPERIMENTAL/CUTTING-EDGE TREATMENTS

### 2.1 Lecanemab (Leqembi) & Donanemab (Kisunla)

**What they are:** Monoclonal antibodies that target and remove beta-amyloid plaques from the brain. These represent the first disease-modifying therapies for Alzheimer's disease.

**Lecanemab (Leqembi -- Eisai/Biogen):**
- FDA traditional approval: January 2023 (full approval July 2023)
- Targets soluble amyloid-beta protofibrils (early-stage aggregates)
- Administration: IV infusion every 2 weeks (~1 hour); maintenance dosing approved Jan 2025 (every 4 weeks IV or weekly subcutaneous)
- CLARITY AD trial results: 27% slowing of cognitive decline vs. placebo over 18 months (statistically significant but clinically debated)
- Reduced amyloid plaque burden by ~60%
- Cost: ~$26,500/year in US
- EMA (Europe): Rejected in July 2024 due to benefit-risk concerns, particularly ARIA risk. **Not currently approved in Sweden/EU.**

**Donanemab (Kisunla -- Eli Lilly):**
- FDA approval: July 2024
- Targets deposited (mature) amyloid-beta plaques (N3pG modification)
- Administration: IV infusion every 4 weeks (~30 min)
- TRAILBLAZER-ALZ 2 trial: 35% slowing of decline in the "low/medium tau" subgroup over 18 months
- Unique feature: treatment can be stopped once amyloid is cleared (typically 6-18 months), unlike lecanemab's indefinite treatment
- About 47% of patients completed treatment (cleared plaques) within 12 months
- Cost: ~$32,000/year in US (but limited treatment duration reduces total cost)
- EMA status: Under review as of late 2025

**Side effects (both):**
- ARIA (Amyloid-Related Imaging Abnormalities): brain swelling (ARIA-E) or microbleeds (ARIA-H) in 20-35% of patients
- Higher risk in ApoE4 homozygotes (2 copies of the gene)
- Usually asymptomatic but can cause headache, confusion, dizziness
- Rare fatal cases reported (especially with blood thinners)
- Requires regular MRI monitoring

**Who qualifies:**
- Early-stage AD only (MCI or mild dementia due to Alzheimer's)
- Must have confirmed elevated amyloid (PET scan or CSF biomarker)
- Not tested/approved for moderate or severe dementia
- Genetic testing for ApoE4 recommended before treatment

**Honest assessment:** These drugs do slow decline, but the absolute clinical benefit is modest -- roughly 4-7 months of delayed progression over 18 months. The debate is whether this is clinically meaningful to patients. They do not restore lost function or cure the disease. The ARIA risk is real. They represent scientific proof-of-concept more than transformative treatment.

**Swedish/Nordic access:** Currently NOT available through standard healthcare in Sweden. EMA rejection of lecanemab means no EU-wide approval. Some patients may access through compassionate use programs or clinical trials. The Karolinska Institute and Swedish BioFINDER study (Lund) are actively involved in amyloid research.

### 2.2 Focused Ultrasound (FUS)

**What it is:** Non-invasive technique using focused ultrasound waves combined with microbubbles (injected IV) to temporarily open the blood-brain barrier (BBB) at targeted locations.

**Mechanism:**
- Microbubbles oscillate in ultrasound field, mechanically loosening tight junctions in the BBB
- BBB opening lasts ~24 hours, allowing drugs, antibodies, or the body's own immune cells to enter the brain
- Can also directly clear amyloid via activated microglia

**Current evidence (2022-2025):**

- A 2023 Canadian study (Bhatt/Bhatt et al., published in NEJM) showed FUS could safely open BBB and reduce amyloid plaques in targeted brain regions in early AD patients -- the first definitive human evidence
- The BBB opening allowed up to 5x more antibody delivery to targeted brain areas
- Phase I/II trials combining FUS with anti-amyloid antibodies are underway
- InSightec (the main device manufacturer) has multiple trials globally
- FUS is already FDA-approved for essential tremor treatment (different application)

**Strength of evidence:** Early-phase, promising. Safety profile looks acceptable so far (BBB opening is reversible). Potential to dramatically enhance drug delivery to the brain.

**Availability:** Research only. Active clinical trials at several sites globally. Check clinicaltrials.gov for enrollment opportunities.

### 2.3 Photobiomodulation (PBM) / Red Light Therapy

**What it is:** Application of near-infrared (NIR) light (wavelengths 600-1100nm, typically 810nm) to the brain, either transcranially (through the skull) or intranasally.

**Proposed mechanisms:**
- Enhances mitochondrial function (cytochrome c oxidase absorbs NIR photons)
- Increases ATP production in neurons
- Reduces oxidative stress and neuroinflammation
- May stimulate BDNF expression
- Increases cerebral blood flow

**Evidence status (2022-2025):**

- Animal studies: Strong. Multiple studies show PBM reduces amyloid plaques, tau pathology, and neuroinflammation in AD mouse models.
- Human studies: Very early. Small pilot studies (n=10-30) have shown:
  - Improved cognitive scores (ADAS-Cog, MMSE) after 12 weeks of daily transcranial PBM
  - Improved sleep and reduced agitation
  - A 2023 RCT (Saltmarche et al.) with helmet-based PBM showed trends toward cognitive improvement but did not reach statistical significance
  - A 2024 Australian trial showed feasibility and safety of home-based PBM in mild-moderate AD

**Honest assessment:** The mechanistic rationale is sound, and animal data is compelling. However, human evidence is limited to small, often unblinded studies. The field suffers from device variability (different wavelengths, power densities, treatment durations) making it hard to compare studies. Not yet evidence-based for clinical recommendation, but low-risk enough that some clinicians/patients pursue it.

**Availability:** PBM devices available commercially (e.g., Vielight Neuro Gamma -- ~$1,800-2,700 USD). Not regulated as medical devices in most countries. Not covered by insurance. Can be used at home.

**Nordic note:** The University of Helsinki has an active photobiomodulation research group. Limited research in Sweden specifically.

### 2.4 Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBOT)

**What it is:** Breathing 100% oxygen at pressures higher than atmospheric (typically 1.5-2.0 ATA) in a pressurized chamber. Sessions typically 60-90 minutes.

**Proposed mechanisms:**
- Dramatically increases dissolved oxygen in blood and tissues
- Promotes angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation) in the brain
- Reduces neuroinflammation
- Enhances neuroplasticity and stem cell mobilization
- May increase cerebral blood flow and oxygen delivery to hypoperfused brain regions

**Evidence (2022-2025):**

- A 2023 Israeli study (Hadanny et al., Tel Aviv) -- the most rigorous to date -- showed 60 sessions of HBOT improved cerebral blood flow and cognitive function in older adults with memory decline. Brain SPECT scans showed increased perfusion.
- A 2024 case series showed improvement in MMSE scores in mild AD patients after 40 sessions
- Animal studies show HBOT reduces amyloid burden and tau phosphorylation
- Multiple small trials show improved cerebral perfusion on imaging

**Honest assessment:** The Israeli group's work is the strongest. Results are encouraging but sample sizes remain small (n=30-60). HBOT is expensive, time-intensive (typically 40-60 sessions, 5 days/week), and not without risk (ear/sinus barotrauma, rarely seizures). The evidence is insufficient for clinical recommendation but worth watching.

**Availability in Sweden:** HBOT chambers exist at several Swedish hospitals (primarily for wound healing, carbon monoxide poisoning, decompression sickness). Off-label use for cognitive indications would require specialist referral. Private HBOT centers are emerging. Cost: ~1,000-2,000 SEK per session.

### 2.5 Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)

**What it is:** Non-invasive brain stimulation using magnetic fields to induce electrical currents in specific brain regions. Repetitive TMS (rTMS) and Theta Burst Stimulation (TBS) are the main protocols.

**Evidence for dementia (2022-2025):**

- A 2024 meta-analysis of 20+ RCTs found rTMS produces statistically significant improvements in global cognition (MMSE, ADAS-Cog) in AD patients, though effect sizes are modest
- The dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) is the most common target
- High-frequency (10-20Hz) stimulation of left DLPFC shows best results
- Neuronavigation-guided TMS (targeting individual brain anatomy) shows better outcomes than standard positioning
- A 2023 multicenter trial showed combined rTMS + cognitive training superior to either alone
- Neuronix BrainsWay developed a specialized helmet-based TMS system for AD (CE marked in Europe)

**Practical details:**
- Typically 20-30 sessions over 4-6 weeks, then maintenance sessions
- Each session: 20-40 minutes
- Generally well tolerated; mild headache is most common side effect
- Rare risk of seizure (<0.1%)

**Availability in Sweden:** TMS is available at several Swedish clinics, primarily for depression (which has TMS approval). Off-label use for dementia would require specialist referral. Some private clinics offer it. The Karolinska Institute has TMS research programs.

### 2.6 Stem Cell Therapy

**What it is:** Various approaches to using stem cells to repair or regenerate damaged brain tissue:
- Mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) -- most commonly studied
- Neural stem cells (NSCs)
- Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs)
- Exosomes/extracellular vesicles from stem cells

**Current status (2022-2025):**

- Multiple Phase I/II trials ongoing globally
- MSCs administered intravenously or intrathecally (spinal injection) appear safe in early trials
- A 2023 Korean trial (Samsung Medical Center) of MSCs in AD showed safety and trends toward slower decline at 12 months
- Mechanism appears to be primarily paracrine (anti-inflammatory signaling) rather than cell replacement
- Exosome therapy (cell-free approach using stem cell secretions) is gaining attention as potentially safer and more practical
- Major challenges: cell survival after transplantation, BBB penetration, scalability, cost

**Honest assessment:** We are far from clinical application. Safety data is accumulating, but efficacy evidence is minimal. The paracrine effect is interesting but modest. The field is heavily promoted by unregulated "stem cell clinics" that charge enormous fees for unproven treatments. Any legitimate therapy is years away from approval.

**Nordic note:** The Karolinska Institute has a strong stem cell biology program but nothing clinically available for dementia yet.

### 2.7 Plasma Exchange / Young Blood (Plasmapheresis)

**What it is:** Therapeutic plasma exchange (TPE) replaces a patient's plasma with albumin solution, removing circulating disease-associated proteins. The "young blood" concept originated from parabiosis experiments showing old mice rejuvenated by young mouse blood.

**Key studies:**

- **AMBAR trial (Grifols, 2022-2024):** The most rigorous study to date. Showed that plasma exchange with albumin replacement slowed cognitive and functional decline by 50-60% in moderate AD over 14 months. This is one of the largest treatment effects seen in any AD trial. Phase III results presented at CTAD 2023 were encouraging.
- **Alkahest/Grifols GRF6021:** Young donor plasma fraction (albumin + key proteins) showed improved functional measures in mild-moderate AD in a Phase II trial
- Proposed mechanism: removes circulating amyloid-beta, inflammatory cytokines, and other "aging factors" from blood; albumin may also bind and clear brain amyloid

**Honest assessment:** The AMBAR results are surprisingly strong and have generated significant interest. However, plasma exchange is resource-intensive (requires regular blood center visits, IV access), expensive, and carries procedural risks. If Phase III confirms the results, this could be a significant treatment option. The albumin+plasma exchange approach is biologically plausible (albumin is the main carrier of amyloid-beta in blood).

**Availability:** Currently research only. Grifols (Spanish pharmaceutical company) is pursuing regulatory approval. Not available in Sweden outside research.

### 2.8 GLP-1 Agonists (Semaglutide/Ozempic, Liraglutide)

**What they are:** Originally developed for type 2 diabetes and obesity. GLP-1 receptor agonists that cross the blood-brain barrier and have direct neuroprotective effects.

**Emerging evidence for dementia (2023-2026):**

- **Large observational studies (2023-2025):** Multiple studies using health registry data show GLP-1 agonist users have 30-50% lower risk of dementia diagnosis compared to other diabetes medications. A 2023 study of 120,000+ patients found semaglutide associated with significantly lower Alzheimer's risk.
- **EVOKE and EVOKE+ trials:** Novo Nordisk's large Phase III RCTs of oral semaglutide (14mg daily) for early AD. ~1,850 participants each. Results expected 2025-2026. These are the gold-standard trials that will definitively answer the question.
- **Liraglutide (ELAD trial, 2024):** Phase IIb trial showed liraglutide slowed loss of brain volume (measured by MRI) in AD patients over 12 months vs. placebo. Cortical glucose metabolism was also better preserved.

**Mechanisms (why a diabetes drug might help the brain):**

- GLP-1 receptors are abundantly expressed in the hippocampus and cortex
- Direct neuroprotective effects: reduces neuroinflammation, oxidative stress, and apoptosis
- Improves brain insulin signaling (AD is sometimes called "type 3 diabetes")
- Reduces tau phosphorylation
- Enhances synaptic plasticity and neurogenesis
- Improves cerebral blood flow
- Weight loss and metabolic improvements may have indirect brain benefits

**Honest assessment:** This is one of the most exciting areas in dementia research. The observational evidence is striking, and the biological mechanisms are compelling. The EVOKE trial results will be pivotal. If positive, GLP-1 agonists would be the most accessible disease-modifying treatment since they're already widely prescribed and well-characterized for safety.

**Availability in Sweden:** Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy/Rybelsus) and liraglutide (Victoza/Saxenda) are prescribed in Sweden for diabetes and obesity. Off-label use for neuroprotection is not standard but theoretically possible. Discuss with a neurologist or endocrinologist. GLP-1 agonists are experiencing severe supply shortages globally.

### 2.9 Rapamycin / mTOR Inhibition

**What it is:** Rapamycin (sirolimus) is an immunosuppressant/anti-aging drug that inhibits the mTOR pathway, a central regulator of cell growth, autophagy, and aging.

**Rationale for dementia:**
- mTOR inhibition activates autophagy -- the cell's self-cleaning mechanism that clears damaged proteins including amyloid-beta and tau
- Reduces neuroinflammation
- Enhances cerebrovascular function
- Strong life-extension data in animal models

**Evidence:**

- Animal studies (multiple): Rapamycin reduces amyloid plaques, tau tangles, and cognitive deficits in AD mouse models, even when given late in disease
- A 2024 pilot study in humans showed low-dose rapamycin (oral) was safe and well-tolerated in older adults and improved some biomarkers of aging
- The VALID trial (2024-ongoing): testing rapamycin for age-related cognitive decline
- No completed RCTs for dementia specifically in humans yet

**Honest assessment:** Theoretically compelling. The autophagy angle is underexplored in dementia therapeutics. However, rapamycin is an immunosuppressant, and long-term use carries infection risk. The optimal dose for "anti-aging" effects (lower than immunosuppression doses) is still being determined. Watch the VALID trial results.

**Availability:** Rapamycin is FDA/EMA-approved as an immunosuppressant (organ transplant). Some longevity-focused physicians prescribe low-dose rapamycin off-label. Available in Sweden by prescription but not indicated for cognitive decline.

### 2.10 Methylene Blue

**What it is:** An old dye/drug (first synthesized 1876) that acts as a mitochondrial electron carrier and antioxidant. Its derivative, LMTM (leuco-methylthioninium, trade name Rember/Hydromethylthionine), has been specifically developed for AD.

**Mechanisms:**
- Inhibits tau aggregation (different target than anti-amyloid drugs)
- Enhances mitochondrial function (accepts and donates electrons in the electron transport chain)
- Reduces oxidative stress
- May improve autophagy

**Evidence:**

- **LUCIDITY trial (TauRx, 2023):** Phase III trial of LMTM (16mg) as monotherapy in mild-moderate AD showed significant slowing of cognitive decline AND reduction in brain atrophy on MRI over 12 months. Importantly, it only worked as monotherapy, not added to acetylcholinesterase inhibitors.
- Effect size was comparable to or larger than anti-amyloid antibodies
- Results controversial -- some researchers question the monotherapy-only benefit
- TauRx is seeking regulatory approval based on LUCIDITY data

**Honest assessment:** If the LUCIDITY results hold up, this is significant -- an oral drug, cheap to manufacture, targeting tau rather than amyloid. The monotherapy-only efficacy is puzzling and has generated skepticism. Regulatory pathway unclear. Worth following closely.

**Availability:** Not approved for AD. Methylene blue itself is available as a medication (for methemoglobinemia) and as a supplement, but the therapeutic form (LMTM) is not commercially available. Self-medication with over-the-counter methylene blue is not recommended without medical guidance.

### 2.11 NAD+ Precursors (NMN, NR)

**What they are:** Nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN) and nicotinamide riboside (NR) are precursors to NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide), a critical coenzyme in cellular energy metabolism that declines with age.

**Rationale for brain aging:**
- NAD+ levels decrease 40-50% in the aging brain
- NAD+ is essential for mitochondrial function, DNA repair (via PARP), and sirtuin activation
- Low NAD+ is linked to neuroinflammation and impaired autophagy
- Restoring NAD+ may protect neurons from metabolic stress

**Evidence (2022-2025):**

- Animal studies: NMN and NR consistently improve cognitive function in aged mice and AD mouse models, reduce neuroinflammation, and enhance cerebrovascular function
- Human studies: Multiple Phase I/II trials show NMN and NR safely raise blood NAD+ levels
- A 2024 clinical trial of NR (1000mg/day) in Parkinson's disease showed improved brain energy metabolism on MRS imaging
- No completed RCTs specifically for AD or dementia in humans yet
- The BRAIN-NR trial (2024-ongoing) is testing NR for age-related cognitive decline

**Honest assessment:** The biological rationale is strong, and NAD+ decline in aging is well-documented. However, it's unclear how much oral supplementation actually raises brain NAD+ (vs. peripheral levels). The supplements are widely available and appear safe, but efficacy for cognitive outcomes is unproven. This is a reasonable "hedge" supplementation strategy with low risk but uncertain benefit.

**Availability:** NMN and NR are available as dietary supplements in Sweden and globally. Not regulated as medicines. Common brands: Tru Niagen (NR), various NMN supplements. Cost: ~500-1500 SEK/month. Quality varies significantly between manufacturers.

### 2.12 Psilocybin / Psychedelics

**What they are:** Psilocybin (from magic mushrooms), LSD, DMT, and other psychedelics that act primarily on serotonin 5-HT2A receptors to produce altered states of consciousness and, importantly, neuroplasticity.

**Potential mechanisms for neurodegeneration:**
- Dramatic stimulation of neuroplasticity (dendritic growth, new synaptic connections)
- Increased BDNF expression
- Reduced neuroinflammation (TNF-alpha, IL-6 reduction)
- Enhanced default mode network flexibility
- Potential stimulation of neurogenesis
- Psychological benefits: reduced depression, anxiety, improved quality of life

**Evidence for dementia (2022-2025):**

- Pre-clinical: DMT and psilocybin promote neurogenesis and neuroplasticity in animal models
- A 2023 Johns Hopkins pilot study explored psilocybin for depression in early-stage AD (safety and feasibility)
- Several Phase I trials are underway or planned for psilocybin in MCI/early AD
- The "micro-dosing" approach (sub-hallucinogenic doses, typically 0.1-0.3g dried mushrooms) is being studied for cognitive enhancement in older adults
- A 2024 review concluded that psychedelic-assisted therapy is feasible in early dementia but evidence for cognitive benefit is essentially absent

**Honest assessment:** The neuroplasticity-promoting effects are real and well-documented. The theoretical case for helping neurodegeneration is interesting. However, there is virtually no clinical evidence of cognitive benefit in dementia patients. Safety concerns exist for this population (confusion, agitation, falls). The psychological benefits (reduced end-of-life anxiety, improved mood) may be the most relevant application. This is very early-stage.

**Nordic note:** Sweden has restrictive drug laws. Psilocybin is classified as a narcotic. Clinical research would require special permissions. No Swedish clinical trials for dementia with psychedelics currently registered.

### 2.13 Cold Exposure / Sauna

**Cold exposure:**

- **Cold shock proteins:** Brief cold exposure (cold water immersion, cold showers) induces expression of RBM3 (RNA-binding motif protein 3), a cold shock protein shown to protect synapses and promote synaptogenesis in hibernating animals and cold-exposed mice
- A landmark 2015 study (Peretti et al., Nature) showed RBM3 prevented neurodegeneration in prion disease mice. Follow-up studies (2023-2024) confirmed the mechanism extends to AD and frontotemporal dementia models
- Human evidence: Observational. Regular winter swimmers have higher RBM3 levels. No RCTs for cognitive outcomes.
- Practical: Cold water immersion at 10-15 degrees C for 2-5 minutes triggers significant RBM3 expression. Cold showers less effective but non-zero.
- **Norepinephrine boost:** Cold exposure increases norepinephrine 2-3x, improving attention and mood acutely

**Sauna / heat exposure:**

- **Heat shock proteins (HSPs):** Sauna bathing induces HSP70 and other chaperone proteins that prevent protein misfolding (including amyloid and tau aggregation)
- **The KIHD study (Finland):** Landmark prospective study (2,300 Finnish men followed 20+ years) showed regular sauna use (4-7x/week) associated with 65% reduced risk of Alzheimer's disease compared to 1x/week users. Published in Age and Ageing, 2017. Follow-up analyses (2023) confirmed the association.
- Mechanisms: improved cardiovascular function, reduced inflammation, increased BDNF, HSP-mediated proteostasis, improved sleep
- Finnish/Swedish sauna tradition makes this highly culturally accessible

**Honest assessment:** The Finnish sauna data is epidemiological (cannot prove causation) but the effect size is enormous and biologically plausible. Cold exposure science is earlier-stage but the RBM3 mechanism is compelling. Both are low-risk, free/cheap, and culturally appropriate in Scandinavia. Worth incorporating as part of a holistic brain health strategy.

### 2.14 Sleep Optimization & the Glymphatic System

**What it is:** The glymphatic system is the brain's waste-clearance system, discovered in 2012 (Nedergaard et al.). During deep sleep, cerebrospinal fluid flows through brain tissue and clears metabolic waste -- including amyloid-beta and tau.

**Key findings (2022-2025):**

- Glymphatic clearance is **most active during deep (N3) sleep** and specifically in the lateral (side) sleeping position
- One night of sleep deprivation increases brain amyloid-beta levels by ~5% (Shokri-Kojori et al.)
- Chronic poor sleep is now considered one of the top modifiable risk factors for Alzheimer's (Lancet Commission 2024)
- Sleep disorders (especially obstructive sleep apnea) are strongly associated with dementia risk
- A 2024 study showed CPAP treatment for sleep apnea reduced amyloid accumulation over 2 years
- Deep sleep specifically (not just total sleep) correlates with amyloid clearance efficiency
- Alcohol, benzodiazepines, and antihistamines suppress deep sleep and may impair glymphatic function

**Practical sleep optimization for brain health:**

1. **Prioritize deep sleep:** 7-8 hours total, with focus on sleep quality (minimize awakenings)
2. **Treat sleep apnea aggressively:** Get a sleep study if snoring, daytime sleepiness, or observed apneas. CPAP/BiPAP significantly reduces dementia risk.
3. **Sleep position:** Side sleeping (lateral decubitus) may optimize glymphatic clearance
4. **Sleep timing:** Consistent sleep-wake schedule; align with circadian rhythm
5. **Temperature:** Cool bedroom (16-18 C) promotes deep sleep
6. **Light exposure:** Bright light in morning, darkness at night; avoid screens 1-2 hours before bed
7. **Avoid deep sleep suppressors:** Limit alcohol (even moderate amounts suppress deep sleep), avoid benzodiazepines if possible
8. **Consider melatonin:** Low-dose (0.5-1mg) melatonin may improve sleep quality in older adults. Higher doses are often counterproductive.

**Swedish resources:** Sovecentrum (sleep centers) in major Swedish hospitals offer sleep studies. Sahlgrenska (Gothenburg) and Karolinska have active sleep-dementia research programs.

## PRACTICAL ACTION PLAN

**What you can do TODAY (no prescription needed):**

1. **Exercise:** 150+ min/week moderate aerobic exercise + 2x resistance training. Dance and dual-task training are bonus. This is the single most evidence-supported intervention.
2. **Sleep:** Prioritize 7-8 hours with focus on deep sleep. Get tested for sleep apnea if any concerns. Sleep on your side.
3. **Sauna:** 4-7x/week traditional sauna if accessible (Finnish-style, 80-100 C, 15-20 min). Culturally ideal for Sweden.
4. **Cold exposure:** Consider regular cold water swimming (kallbad) or cold showers. Supports RBM3 expression.
5. **Cognitive engagement:** Combined with exercise (dual-task) is ideal. Social activity is independently protective.

**What to discuss with a doctor:**

1. **GLP-1 agonists:** If metabolic risk factors (diabetes, obesity, prediabetes), discuss semaglutide/liraglutide with awareness of cognitive benefit potential
2. **Sleep apnea screening:** If snoring, daytime fatigue, or observed apneas
3. **Clinical trial participation:** Contact BioFINDER (Lund), Karolinska, or your local memory clinic
4. **TMS:** For diagnosed dementia, ask about rTMS availability at your hospital

**Worth considering (supplements, lower evidence):**

1. NAD+ precursors (NR or NMN)
2. Omega-3 fatty acids (DHA specifically)
3. Vitamin D (if deficient -- common in Nordic countries)
4. Melatonin (low-dose, for sleep optimization)
