## 1. Ketogenic Diet / MCT Oil

### What It Is
A very low-carbohydrate, high-fat diet (typically <30g carbs/day) that forces the body into ketosis, producing ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate) as an alternative fuel. MCT (medium-chain triglyceride) oil is a concentrated fat source from coconut/palm kernel oil that rapidly converts to ketones without requiring full dietary ketosis.

### Mechanism of Action
- **Metabolic bypass**: Alzheimer's brains show ~25-45% reduced glucose uptake ("Type 3 diabetes" hypothesis). Ketones bypass impaired glucose metabolism and provide an alternative energy substrate directly to neurons.
- **Beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB)**: Acts as a signaling molecule beyond fuel -- inhibits NLRP3 inflammasome (reducing neuroinflammation), enhances BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), improves mitochondrial function, and reduces oxidative stress.
- **Reduced insulin resistance**: Ketosis improves insulin signaling in the brain, which is impaired in AD.
- **Amyloid reduction**: Preclinical studies show ketones reduce amyloid-beta production and improve amyloid clearance.

### Strength of Evidence: **MODERATE**

**Key Studies:**
- **Fortier et al. (2021)** -- BENEFIC Trial, Alzheimer's & Dementia: 6-month RCT in 83 patients with MCI. 30g/day MCT supplement improved brain ketone uptake, episodic memory, language, executive function, and processing speed vs. placebo. Brain energy (PET imaging) significantly improved. *This is one of the strongest trials to date.*
- **Phillips et al. (2021)** -- Alzheimer's Research & Therapy: 12-week crossover RCT in 26 AD patients. Modified ketogenic diet improved daily function (ADCS-ADL scores) and quality of life vs. usual diet. No cognitive test improvement, possibly due to short duration.
- **Henderson et al. (2009)** -- Nutrition & Metabolism: AC-1202 (MCT-derived caprylic triglyceride) improved cognition in APOE4-negative AD patients but not APOE4 carriers after 90 days.
- **Xu et al. (2023)** -- Systematic review, Advances in Nutrition: Confirmed MCT supplementation raises ketone levels and shows cognitive benefits in MCI/early AD across multiple trials.
- **Neth et al. (2020)** -- Journal of Alzheimer's Disease: Modified Mediterranean-Ketogenic Diet (MMKD) in 20 adults at risk for AD improved CSF AD biomarkers (amyloid-beta 42) after 6 weeks vs. low-fat diet.

### Practical Protocol
- **MCT Oil route (easier)**: Start with 5g/day, titrate up to 15-30g/day over 2-4 weeks. Take with meals. C8 (caprylic acid) is most ketogenic. Common brands: Bulletproof C8, NOW MCT Oil.
- **Full ketogenic diet**: <20-30g net carbs/day, 70-80% fat, 15-20% protein. Requires medical supervision for elderly patients. Monitor weight, nutrient intake, kidney function.
- **Key caution**: GI side effects (diarrhea, nausea) common initially. APOE4 carriers may respond differently to high-fat diets -- conflicting data.

## 2. Mediterranean & MIND Diets

### What They Are
- **Mediterranean diet**: Rich in olive oil, fish, vegetables, fruits, nuts, legumes, whole grains; moderate wine; low red meat and processed food.
- **MIND diet** (Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay): Hybrid of Mediterranean + DASH diets specifically designed for brain health. Created by Martha Clare Morris at Rush University (2015). Emphasizes: green leafy vegetables (6+/week), other vegetables (1+/day), berries (2+/week), nuts (5+/week), olive oil, whole grains (3+/day), fish (1+/week), beans (3+/week), poultry (2+/week), wine (1 glass/day). Limits: butter (<1 tbsp/day), cheese (<1/week), red meat (<4/week), fried food (<1/week), pastries (<5/week).

### Mechanism of Action
- Anti-inflammatory polyphenols and antioxidants reduce chronic neuroinflammation
- Omega-3 fatty acids support neuronal membrane integrity and reduce amyloid aggregation
- Folate, B-vitamins, and vitamin E reduce homocysteine (neurotoxic) levels
- Polyphenols (from olive oil, berries, wine) activate Nrf2 antioxidant pathways and enhance autophagy
- Nitrate-rich vegetables improve cerebrovascular blood flow

### Strength of Evidence: **STRONG**

**Key Studies:**
- **Morris et al. (2015)** -- Alzheimer's & Dementia: Observational study in 923 older adults followed 4.5 years. Highest MIND diet adherence reduced AD risk by 53%. Even moderate adherence reduced risk by 35%. MIND diet was more protective than Mediterranean or DASH alone.
- **PREDIMED-Plus trial (2022-2023)** -- Multiple publications: Mediterranean diet supplemented with EVOO or nuts improved cognitive function over 6.5 years in elderly at cardiovascular risk (n=6,874).
- **Barnes et al. (2023)** -- MIND diet RCT (MIND Trial): 604 cognitively healthy older adults randomized to MIND diet vs. mild calorie-restricted control diet for 3 years. MIND diet did NOT significantly reduce cognitive decline vs. control in this RCT. However, both groups ate healthily. This challenges the observational data but may reflect a "healthy volunteer" floor effect.
- **Agarwal et al. (2023)** -- Journal of Alzheimer's Disease: MIND diet associated with fewer AD pathological hallmarks (amyloid plaques, tau tangles) in brain autopsy data from 581 participants.
- **Shannon et al. (2023)** -- Meta-analysis, Ageing Research Reviews: Mediterranean diet adherence associated with 20-35% reduced risk of cognitive impairment across pooled studies.

### Practical Protocol
- Follow MIND diet guidelines (10 brain-healthy food groups, 5 unhealthy groups to limit)
- **Minimum effective dose**: Even moderate adherence shows benefit
- No calorie counting required -- focus on food group targets
- The most practical and sustainable dietary intervention for dementia prevention

## 3. Intermittent Fasting / Time-Restricted Eating

### What It Is
- **Time-restricted eating (TRE)**: Eating within a 6-10 hour window daily
- **5:2 fasting**: Normal eating 5 days, ~500-600 cal on 2 non-consecutive days
- **Alternate-day fasting (ADF)**: Alternating normal and very low-calorie days

### Mechanism of Action
- **Autophagy activation**: Fasting (typically >14-16 hours) activates cellular cleanup, clearing misfolded proteins including amyloid-beta and tau. Mediated by AMPK activation and mTOR inhibition.
- **BDNF upregulation**: Fasting increases brain-derived neurotrophic factor 2-3 fold, supporting neurogenesis and synaptic plasticity.
- **Neuroinflammation reduction**: Fasting reduces circulating pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha, CRP) and microglial activation.
- **Ketone production**: Mild ketosis during fasting provides alternative brain fuel (see Section 1).
- **Improved insulin sensitivity**: Reduces brain insulin resistance, a hallmark of AD.
- **Circadian rhythm optimization**: TRE aligns eating with circadian rhythms, improving sleep and melatonin production (sleep disruption accelerates amyloid deposition).

### Strength of Evidence: **MODERATE (mostly preclinical, growing human data)**

**Key Studies:**
- **Mattson et al. (2018)** -- New England Journal of Medicine review: Comprehensive review of intermittent fasting mechanisms for brain health. Strong preclinical evidence for neuroprotection through autophagy and BDNF pathways.
- **Ooi et al. (2024)** -- Ageing Research Reviews: Systematic review. Intermittent fasting in animal models of AD consistently shows reduced amyloid pathology, improved cognition, and reduced neuroinflammation. Human evidence is preliminary but promising.
- **Gudden et al. (2021)** -- Nutrients: TRE improved cognitive function in healthy older adults (small pilot, n=40).
- **Halagappa et al. (2007, updated by subsequent studies)** -- Foundational preclinical work showing alternate-day fasting protects against excitotoxic and metabolic neuronal damage in mice.
- **No large RCTs in dementia patients yet** -- this is the main limitation.

### Practical Protocol
- **16:8 TRE** is the most practical: eat between ~10am-6pm. Start with 12:10 and extend gradually.
- **Key cautions for elderly**: Risk of malnutrition and sarcopenia. Must maintain adequate protein (1.0-1.2g/kg/day) and total calories. Not suitable for underweight patients or those with dysphagia.
- Early time-restricted eating (finishing eating by 6pm) may be superior due to circadian alignment.
- Consult physician before starting -- especially for diabetics on medication (hypoglycemia risk).

## 4. Specific Foods with Strong Evidence

### Blueberries & Berries
- **Mechanism**: Anthocyanins cross BBB, reduce neuroinflammation, enhance neuronal signaling, improve cerebral blood flow, activate CREB-BDNF pathway.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE-STRONG**. Krikorian et al. (2022, Nutrients) -- 12-week RCT in 86 older adults with subjective cognitive decline: daily wild blueberry powder reduced executive function decline and improved metabolic function. Multiple RCTs show improved memory and processing speed. The MIND diet emphasizes berries specifically (2+ servings/week).
- **Dose**: 1 cup (150g) fresh blueberries daily or 1-2 tablespoons wild blueberry powder.

### Fatty Fish (Omega-3 DHA)
- **Mechanism**: DHA is the primary structural fatty acid in brain cell membranes (~40% of brain polyunsaturated fats). Reduces amyloid-beta production, enhances amyloid clearance, anti-inflammatory via resolvins and protectins, improves synaptic plasticity.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE**. Observational data strongly links fish consumption (2+/week) with ~30% reduced dementia risk. RCTs of fish oil supplements have been disappointing, possibly because supplementation cannot reverse decades of deficiency. APOE4 carriers may need higher doses. Best evidence is for DHA in pre-dementia stages.
- **Dose**: 2-3 servings fatty fish/week (salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring). If supplementing: 1000-2000mg DHA/day. Whole fish > supplements.

### Turmeric / Curcumin
- **Mechanism**: Curcumin binds amyloid-beta directly and inhibits plaque formation, potent anti-inflammatory (NF-kB inhibition), antioxidant, enhances BDNF, crosses BBB (in low amounts).
- **Evidence**: **EARLY-MODERATE**. Epidemiological data: India (high turmeric consumption) has among the lowest AD rates globally. Small et al. (2018, Am J Geriatric Psychiatry) -- 18-month RCT: Theracurmin (highly bioavailable curcumin, 90mg 2x/day) improved memory and attention vs. placebo in non-demented adults, with PET showing reduced amyloid and tau in brain. Major limitation: poor bioavailability of standard curcumin. Bioavailable forms (Theracurmin, Meriva, Longvida, BCM-95) required.
- **Dose**: 500-1000mg bioavailable curcumin 2x/day with fat and black pepper (piperine increases absorption 2000%). Or liberal turmeric use in cooking with fat and pepper.

### Green Tea (EGCG)
- **Mechanism**: EGCG (epigallocatechin-3-gallate) inhibits amyloid-beta aggregation, chelates metal ions (iron, copper) involved in oxidative damage, anti-inflammatory, promotes autophagy, neuroprotective.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE (epidemiological + preclinical)**. Large Japanese cohort studies consistently show 2+ cups green tea/day associated with ~50% reduced cognitive decline risk. Noguchi-Shinohara et al. (2014, PLOS ONE): Green tea consumption inversely associated with cognitive decline in elderly Japanese (n=723). Few human clinical trials specifically for dementia.
- **Dose**: 3-5 cups green tea/day (brewing 2-3 min at 70-80C preserves EGCG) or 200-400mg EGCG supplement.

### Dark Chocolate / Cocoa
- **Mechanism**: Cocoa flavanols improve endothelial function, increase cerebral blood flow, reduce neuroinflammation, lower blood pressure, improve insulin sensitivity.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE**. Brickman et al. (2023, PNAS) -- COSMOS-Mind study (ancillary to COSMOS): Cocoa flavanol supplementation (500mg/day) for 3 years did not improve overall cognition in 2,262 older adults, but significantly improved cognition in those with poor baseline diet. Desideri et al. (2012, Hypertension): 8-week RCT showed high-flavanol cocoa improved cognitive function in MCI patients.
- **Dose**: 30-50g dark chocolate (>70% cocoa) daily or cocoa flavanol supplement (500mg/day).

### Extra Virgin Olive Oil (EVOO)
- **Mechanism**: Oleocanthal has ibuprofen-like anti-inflammatory properties and enhances amyloid clearance across BBB. Hydroxytyrosol is a potent antioxidant. Polyphenols reduce tau aggregation. Monounsaturated fats improve vascular health.
- **Evidence**: **STRONG (within Mediterranean diet context)**. PREDIMED trial: EVOO-supplemented Mediterranean diet improved cognition and reduced MCI incidence. Abuznait et al. (2013, ACS Chemical Neuroscience): Oleocanthal enhances amyloid-beta clearance across BBB. Multiple epidemiological studies link EVOO consumption with reduced AD risk.
- **Dose**: 2-4 tablespoons/day as primary cooking oil and dressing. Must be genuine EVOO (cold-pressed, in dark bottle, harvest date visible).

### Walnuts
- **Mechanism**: Highest omega-3 (ALA) content among nuts, polyphenols (ellagic acid, ellagitannins), melatonin, folate, vitamin E. Reduce oxidative stress and neuroinflammation.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE (mostly preclinical + observational)**. Chauhan et al. (2020, Nutrients): Walnut supplementation in transgenic AD mice reduced amyloid-beta, oxidative stress, and improved memory. Arab & Ang (2015, J Nutr Health Aging): NHANES analysis showed walnut consumers scored significantly higher on cognitive tests regardless of age, sex, ethnicity.
- **Dose**: 1-2 ounces (28-56g) daily, roughly a handful.

## 5. Gut-Brain Axis

### What It Is
The bidirectional communication network between gut microbiota and the brain via the vagus nerve, immune signaling, microbial metabolites (short-chain fatty acids), and the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis. Emerging as one of the most important frontiers in dementia research.

### Mechanism of Action
- **Neuroinflammation**: Gut dysbiosis increases intestinal permeability ("leaky gut"), allowing LPS (lipopolysaccharide) and pro-inflammatory cytokines into the bloodstream and across the BBB, driving chronic neuroinflammation -- a central feature of AD.
- **Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)**: Butyrate, propionate, acetate (produced by gut bacteria from fiber) are anti-inflammatory, strengthen gut barrier, and cross BBB to modulate microglial activation and reduce neuroinflammation.
- **Amyloid production**: Certain gut bacteria produce amyloid proteins (curli fibers from E. coli) that may cross-seed brain amyloid aggregation via prion-like mechanisms.
- **Neurotransmitter production**: >90% of serotonin and significant GABA, dopamine produced in gut. Dysbiosis alters neurotransmitter balance.
- **Bile acid metabolism**: Gut bacteria modify bile acids, which regulate brain inflammation and tau pathology.

### Strength of Evidence: **MODERATE-STRONG (mechanistic); EARLY (interventional)**

**Key Studies:**
- **Vogt et al. (2017)** -- Scientific Reports: AD patients have significantly altered gut microbiome composition vs. controls -- reduced diversity and specific taxa changes (reduced Firmicutes, increased Bacteroidetes).
- **Cattaneo et al. (2017)** -- Neurobiology of Aging: Gut bacterial amyloid and LPS levels correlate with brain amyloid burden and peripheral inflammatory markers in elderly.
- **Leblhuber et al. (2021)** -- Journal of Alzheimer's Disease: Probiotic supplementation (Omni-Biotic Stress Repair) improved tryptophan metabolism and reduced inflammatory markers in AD patients.
- **Akbari et al. (2016)** -- Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience: 12-week RCT of probiotic milk (L. acidophilus, L. casei, B. bifidum, L. fermentum) in 60 AD patients significantly improved MMSE cognitive scores and reduced inflammatory/metabolic markers (CRP, triglycerides, insulin resistance).
- **Kim et al. (2024)** -- Alzheimer's Research & Therapy: Systematic review confirming gut microbiome alterations in AD and linking specific microbial signatures to disease progression.

### Practical Protocol
- **Probiotic foods**: Kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, miso, natto, yogurt (live cultures), kombucha. Aim for variety of fermented foods daily.
- **Prebiotic fiber**: 25-35g fiber/day from diverse sources -- garlic, onions, leeks, asparagus, artichokes, bananas, oats, flaxseed. Diversity of fiber sources is more important than any single source.
- **Probiotic supplements**: Multi-strain formulas containing Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species, 10-50 billion CFU/day. Evidence is still emerging for optimal strains.
- **Avoid**: Unnecessary antibiotics, artificial sweeteners (disrupt microbiome), highly processed food, emulsifiers (polysorbate 80, carboxymethylcellulose).

## 6. Supplements with Clinical Evidence

### Lion's Mane Mushroom (Hericium erinaceus)
- **Mechanism**: Contains hericenones and erinacines that stimulate nerve growth factor (NGF) synthesis. NGF promotes neuronal growth, survival, and myelination. Also anti-inflammatory and antioxidant.
- **Evidence**: **EARLY**. Mori et al. (2009, Phytother Res) -- RCT in 30 MCI patients: 16 weeks of Lion's Mane (3g/day) improved cognitive function vs. placebo, but benefits disappeared after discontinuation. Li et al. (2020, Front Aging Neurosci) -- Pilot RCT in AD patients: 49 weeks of erinacine A-enriched Lion's Mane improved daily living activities but not cognition. ADDF Cognitive Vitality rating (updated Sep 2025): Mixed clinical evidence, preclinical promising. Larger trials needed.
- **Dose**: 1-3g/day dried fruiting body or 500-1000mg extract standardized to hericenones/erinacines. Quality varies enormously between brands.
- **Caution**: Limited long-term safety data. One case report of acute respiratory distress. Not standardized.

### Omega-3 DHA/EPA
- **Mechanism**: See fatty fish section. DHA specifically: membrane fluidity, synaptic function, resolvin production (anti-inflammatory). EPA: primarily anti-inflammatory.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE for prevention, WEAK for treatment**. Yurko-Mauro et al. (2010, Alzheimer's & Dementia) -- 24-week RCT, 900mg DHA/day improved memory in age-related cognitive decline (n=485). LipiDiDiet trial (2017-2021): Souvenaid (containing DHA/EPA + uridine + choline + B-vitamins + other) slowed brain atrophy in prodromal AD over 3 years. However, standalone omega-3 trials in established dementia mostly negative.
- **Dose**: 1000-2000mg DHA/day (not just "fish oil" -- check DHA content). Combined DHA+EPA 2000-3000mg/day.

### Phosphatidylserine (PS)
- **Mechanism**: Major phospholipid in neuronal cell membranes. Supports membrane fluidity, neurotransmitter release, and signal transduction. Declines with age.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE (older studies)**. Multiple RCTs from 1990s-2000s showed 300mg/day PS improved memory and cognitive function in age-related decline and early dementia. Kang et al. (2014, meta-analysis) confirmed benefits. Modern trials limited.
- **Dose**: 100-300mg/day. Soy-derived PS is most studied. Often combined with DHA.

### Vitamin D
- **Mechanism**: Neurosteroid functions, regulates calcium signaling, reduces amyloid-beta neurotoxicity, anti-inflammatory, supports BBB integrity. Vitamin D receptors abundant in hippocampus.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE**. Littlejohns et al. (2014, Neurology): Severely deficient adults (<25 nmol/L) had 122% increased risk of dementia (n=1,658, 5.6-year follow-up). Ghahremani et al. (2023, Alzheimer's & Dementia Real World Evidence): Retrospective study of 12,388 participants -- vitamin D supplementation associated with 40% lower dementia incidence, especially in women and APOE4 non-carriers. However, no large RCT has confirmed causality.
- **Dose**: Test blood levels. Target 40-60 ng/mL (100-150 nmol/L). Typical supplementation: 2000-5000 IU/day vitamin D3.

### Vitamin B12 / Folate / B6
- **Mechanism**: Deficiency causes elevated homocysteine, which is directly neurotoxic, promotes vascular damage, and accelerates brain atrophy. B-vitamins lower homocysteine via remethylation to methionine.
- **Evidence**: **STRONG for those with elevated homocysteine**. VITACOG trial (Smith et al., 2010, PLOS ONE): High-dose B-vitamins (B6 20mg, B12 500mcg, folate 800mcg) reduced brain atrophy rate by 30% over 2 years in MCI patients with elevated homocysteine. Effect was 53% reduction in those with highest homocysteine. Subsequent analysis showed this atrophy reduction was mediated by omega-3 DHA levels -- B-vitamins + adequate omega-3 had the strongest effect.
- **Dose**: B12: 500-1000mcg methylcobalamin; Folate: 400-800mcg methylfolate (NOT folic acid -- some people can't convert it); B6: 20-50mg P5P. Test homocysteine and B12 levels first.

### Magnesium Threonate (Magtein)
- **Mechanism**: Only magnesium form shown to significantly cross the BBB and elevate brain magnesium levels. Enhances synaptic density, NMDA receptor function, and learning/memory. Magnesium deficiency is common in elderly (~60%).
- **Evidence**: **EARLY-MODERATE**. Slutsky et al. (2010, Neuron): Magnesium threonate enhanced learning, working memory, and short/long-term memory in rats by increasing synaptic density. Liu et al. (2023, European Journal of Clinical Nutrition): RCT of magnesium threonate improved cognitive function in older adults with subjective cognitive decline. Limited large human trials.
- **Dose**: 1-2g magnesium threonate/day (provides ~144mg elemental magnesium). Often taken in evening (also supports sleep). Magtein is the branded form.

### NAC (N-Acetylcysteine)
- **Mechanism**: Precursor to glutathione (brain's primary antioxidant, depleted in AD). Also modulates glutamate neurotransmission, reduces oxidative stress, chelates metals, anti-inflammatory.
- **Evidence**: **EARLY-MODERATE**. Adair et al. (2001, Neurology): 24-week RCT in AD patients showed trend toward cognitive improvement. Largely studied as part of combination approaches (e.g., nutraceutical "cocktails"). Strong mechanistic rationale but limited standalone clinical evidence.
- **Dose**: 600-1200mg 2x/day. Take on empty stomach. Pairs well with vitamin C.

### Alpha-Lipoic Acid (ALA)
- **Mechanism**: Potent antioxidant, chelates heavy metals, regenerates other antioxidants (glutathione, vitamin C, E), improves glucose metabolism, crosses BBB, reduces NF-kB activation.
- **Evidence**: **EARLY-MODERATE**. Hager et al. (2007, J Neural Transm): 48-month open-label study: 600mg/day ALA slowed cognitive decline in AD patients. Usually studied in combination with other supplements. Shinto et al. (2014, J Alzheimers Dis): ALA + DHA combination slowed cognitive and functional decline in AD over 12 months vs. placebo.
- **Dose**: 300-600mg R-lipoic acid (R-form preferred, more bioactive) 1-2x/day on empty stomach.

### CoQ10 / Ubiquinol
- **Mechanism**: Essential mitochondrial co-factor for energy production. Potent antioxidant. Brain mitochondrial dysfunction is an early feature of AD. CoQ10 levels decline with age and statin use.
- **Evidence**: **EARLY**. Strong preclinical evidence: CoQ10 reduces oxidative damage and amyloid burden in AD mouse models. Limited human clinical trials specifically for dementia. One small trial showed MitoQ (mitochondria-targeted CoQ10) reduced biomarkers of oxidative stress but no cognitive outcomes. Mainly extrapolated from cardiovascular and general aging data.
- **Dose**: 200-400mg ubiquinol (reduced form, better absorbed than ubiquinone) per day with fat. Higher doses for those on statins.

### Resveratrol
- **Mechanism**: Activates SIRT1 (longevity pathway), promotes autophagy, anti-inflammatory, reduces amyloid-beta levels and tau hyperphosphorylation, improves cerebral blood flow, mimics caloric restriction.
- **Evidence**: **EARLY-MODERATE**. Turner et al. (2015, Neurology): 52-week RCT in 119 mild-moderate AD patients: 1g resveratrol 2x/day stabilized CSF amyloid-beta 40 levels (which normally decline as disease progresses) and reduced MMP-9 (neuroinflammation marker). No cognitive improvement. Bioavailability is a major issue. Moussa et al. (2017, J Alzheimers Dis): Follow-up confirming biomarker changes.
- **Dose**: 500mg-1000mg 2x/day trans-resveratrol. Bioavailability enhanced by piperine/quercetin. Pterostilbene (related compound) may have 4x better bioavailability.

## 7. Foods & Substances to AVOID

### Sugar & Refined Carbohydrates
- **Mechanism**: Chronic hyperglycemia causes insulin resistance in the brain ("Type 3 diabetes"), promotes AGEs (advanced glycation end products) that crosslink amyloid and tau, drives neuroinflammation, impairs synaptic plasticity.
- **Evidence**: **STRONG**. Diabetics have 50-100% increased AD risk. High-glycemic diets associated with faster cognitive decline. Crane et al. (2013, NEJM): Even normal-range higher blood glucose associated with increased dementia risk.
- **Limit**: Added sugar <25g/day. Avoid sugary drinks entirely. Minimize white bread, white rice, pastries.

### Ultra-Processed Food
- **Mechanism**: High in added sugars, seed oils, emulsifiers, artificial additives. Promotes systemic inflammation, disrupts gut microbiome, provides caloric excess without micronutrients. Emulsifiers (polysorbate 80, carrageenan) directly damage gut barrier.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE-STRONG**. Goncalves et al. (2023, JAMA Neurology): In ~10,000 adults followed 8+ years, higher ultra-processed food intake was associated with 25% faster cognitive decline. Li et al. (2022, Neurology): Similar findings in UK Biobank (n=72,083), with replacement of processed food with unprocessed food reversing the association.
- **Limit**: Minimize packaged/processed food. Whole foods as much as possible.

### Industrial Seed Oils (Omega-6 Excess)
- **Mechanism**: Soybean, corn, canola, sunflower, safflower oils are high in omega-6 linoleic acid. Excess omega-6 relative to omega-3 (modern ratio ~15:1 vs. ancestral ~1:1-4:1) promotes pro-inflammatory eicosanoid production (PGE2, thromboxanes, leukotrienes) driving chronic neuroinflammation.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE (mechanistic + epidemiological)**. No RCTs directly testing seed oil removal for dementia. However, strong evidence that high omega-6:omega-3 ratio promotes inflammation and that reducing this ratio improves inflammatory markers. Mediterranean diets that show brain benefits primarily use olive oil.
- **Replace with**: Extra virgin olive oil, avocado oil, coconut oil, butter/ghee.

### Aluminum
- **Mechanism**: Aluminum accumulates in brain tissue, promotes amyloid-beta aggregation, and generates reactive oxygen species. Found in some antacids, antiperspirants, cookware, and processed food additives.
- **Evidence**: **WEAK-MODERATE**. Epidemiological association between high aluminum exposure (e.g., drinking water) and AD risk exists but is inconsistent. Exley et al. (2014, Front Neurol): Familial AD cases showed high brain aluminum at autopsy. The aluminum-AD link remains controversial and is not considered causative by most researchers.
- **Practical**: Avoid cooking acidic food in aluminum. Use aluminum-free deodorant. Check antacid ingredients. Probably low priority relative to other dietary interventions.

### Specific Harmful Compounds
- **Acrylamide**: Formed when starchy foods are heated to >120C (frying, baking). Neurotoxic in animal studies. Found in chips, fries, toast, coffee. Minimize deep-frying and charring.
- **Advanced Glycation End Products (AGEs)**: Formed during high-temperature cooking (grilling, frying, roasting). Crosslink proteins, promote inflammation. Cook with moist heat methods (steaming, poaching, stewing) when possible.
- **Nitrosamines**: Formed from nitrites in processed meats. de la Monte et al. (2009, J Alzheimers Dis) proposed nitrosamines contribute to insulin resistance and AD. Limit cured/processed meats.
- **Excess alcohol**: >14 units/week accelerates brain atrophy and cognitive decline. 1 glass wine/day (MIND diet) has different profile vs. heavy drinking.

## 8. Emerging Pharmacological Research

### GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (Semaglutide/Ozempic, Liraglutide, etc.)
- **What**: Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists, developed for type 2 diabetes and obesity. GLP-1 receptors are expressed throughout the brain, particularly in hippocampus.
- **Mechanism**: Reduces brain insulin resistance, anti-inflammatory (reduces microglial activation), neuroprotective (reduces oxidative stress, promotes neuronal survival), improves BBB integrity, may reduce amyloid and tau pathology. Also reduces cardiovascular risk factors (obesity, diabetes, hypertension) which independently increase dementia risk.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE-STRONG (observational); EARLY (interventional)**
  - Norgaard et al. (2022, Alzheimer's & Dementia): Danish nationwide study -- GLP-1 RA use in diabetics associated with significantly lower dementia risk vs. other diabetes drugs.
  - Cukierman-Yaffe et al. (2020, Lancet Neurology): REWIND trial (dulaglutide) -- cognitive sub-study showed GLP-1 RA reduced cognitive decline in type 2 diabetics over 5.4 years.
  - Novo Nordisk launched the EVOKE/EVOKE+ Phase 3 trials of semaglutide for early AD (estimated completion ~2025-2026). These are the first large RCTs directly testing GLP-1 RA for AD.
  - Multiple large observational studies (UK Biobank, US VA data, 2023-2025) consistently show 35-55% reduced dementia risk in GLP-1 RA users vs. other diabetes treatments.
- **Status**: Cannot yet recommend for dementia specifically. Awaiting EVOKE trial results. If positive, this would be one of the most significant AD breakthroughs.

### Rapamycin / Rapalogs
- **What**: mTOR inhibitor. Originally an immunosuppressant. Now studied as a longevity/anti-aging compound.
- **Mechanism**: Inhibits mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin), which activates autophagy -- the cell's cleanup system that clears misfolded proteins (amyloid-beta, tau). Also reduces neuroinflammation, improves cerebrovascular function, enhances lysosomal function.
- **Evidence**: **EARLY (preclinical strong, human data very limited)**. Caccamo et al. (2010, Journal of Biological Chemistry): Rapamycin reduced amyloid and tau pathology and improved cognition in 3xTg-AD mice. Multiple preclinical studies confirm this. The REACH trial (rapamycin for Alzheimer's) and other small human trials are underway. Matt Kaeberlein's PEARL trial tested rapamycin in healthy older adults for aging biomarkers.
- **Dose**: Typically 3-6mg/week in aging studies (pulsed, not daily). Significant side effects at higher doses (immunosuppression, metabolic effects). NOT for self-experimentation.

### Metformin
- **What**: First-line type 2 diabetes drug for 60+ years. The most-studied drug for potential anti-aging effects.
- **Mechanism**: AMPK activation (energy sensing), reduces insulin resistance, anti-inflammatory, reduces mTOR signaling (promotes autophagy), may reduce tau phosphorylation, improves mitochondrial function.
- **Evidence**: **MODERATE (mixed)**. Large observational studies show conflicting results:
  - Shi et al. (2019, Cochrane/meta-analysis): Metformin use in diabetics associated with reduced dementia risk in most but not all studies.
  - However, Campbell et al. (2018, Diabetes Care): Long-term metformin use associated with B12 deficiency, which itself causes cognitive decline. Must supplement B12.
  - TAME trial (Targeting Aging with Metformin) is the landmark anti-aging trial but does not specifically focus on dementia.
  - Luchsinger et al. (2016, J Alzheimers Dis): Pilot RCT of metformin in MCI (non-diabetic) -- improved recall and executive function, with improved CSF biomarkers.
- **Dose**: 500-1000mg 2x/day (standard diabetes dose). Must monitor B12. Extended-release formulation better tolerated.

## Practical Priority Protocol (If Starting Today)

**Tier 1 -- Highest priority, start immediately:**
1. Adopt MIND/Mediterranean diet pattern
2. Use EVOO as primary fat
3. Test and correct B12, folate, vitamin D deficiency
4. Eat fatty fish 2-3x/week
5. Eat berries daily, especially blueberries
6. Eliminate ultra-processed food and excess sugar
7. Include diverse fermented foods daily
