# Iran Naval Blockade: Secondary & Non-Obvious Investment Opportunities

**Date:** April 17, 2026
**Context:** Following the Feb 28, 2026 US-Israel strikes on Iran ("Operation Epic Fury"), Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz on March 2. Brent crude surged from $61/b to $118/b in Q1 2026 -- the largest inflation-adjusted quarterly increase since 1988. Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and UAE shut in production. VLCC tanker rates hit multi-decade highs. The Brent-WTI spread blew out to $25/b on March 31.

---

## 1. Oilfield Services -- The Picks & Shovels

### The Thesis
With Hormuz closed and Middle East production shut in, the world needs US production to fill the gap. US crude already hit a record 13.6M b/d in 2025. Accelerating drilling requires more rigs, more completions, more services. OFS companies have pricing power for the first time since 2022.

### Top Pick: **Halliburton (HAL)**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Exposure | ~60% North America revenue (highest NA exposure among Big 3) |
| Why now | Completions-heavy business model. When US operators drill faster, HAL benefits first -- they dominate US fracking/completions |
| Pricing power | Already reported service price increases in Q4 2025; Hormuz closure accelerates this |
| Valuation | Historically trades at 8-12x forward earnings; likely at low end given pre-crisis uncertainty, meaning upside on re-rating |
| Dividend yield | ~2.0% |
| Risk | If crisis resolves quickly, drilling acceleration stalls |

**Why not SLB:** SLB has more international/Middle East exposure (~75% international revenue). Middle East operations are currently disrupted -- Iraq declared force majeure on foreign-operated oilfields. HAL's NA-heavy business is the direct beneficiary.

### Runner-up: **Liberty Energy (LBRT)**

Pure-play US completions/fracking. Smallest of the group, most leveraged to US activity uptick. Higher beta play. ~1.5% dividend yield.

## 2. Pipeline / Midstream -- Toll Roads on Higher Volumes

### The Thesis
US production is at record highs and climbing. US LNG exports forecast to grow ~30% by 2027 with 5 new facilities coming online. Midstream companies are volume-based toll collectors -- more barrels and MCF moving through pipes = more revenue. They don't bear commodity price risk directly. High dividend yields provide downside protection.

### Top Pick: **Energy Transfer (ET)**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Network | Largest diversified US midstream: 125,000+ miles of pipeline, NGL, crude, nat gas, refined products |
| LNG exposure | Direct ownership in Lake Charles LNG (under development); feeder pipelines to Gulf Coast LNG terminals |
| NGL exports | US NGL exports hit record 3.1M b/d in 2025 and growing. ET is the largest NGL pipeline operator |
| Dividend yield | ~7.5-8% |
| Valuation | Trades at ~8x EV/EBITDA, below historical average |
| Catalysts | Volume growth from Permian/Haynesville; potential Jones Act waiver boosting Gulf Coast throughput |

**Why not EPD:** Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) is the "safer" play (~7% yield, investment-grade, 25+ years of distribution increases). But ET has more upside leverage to the current crisis because of its crude oil pipeline network and LNG positioning. EPD is the sleep-at-night pick; ET is the higher-return pick.

### Runner-up: **Williams Companies (WMB)**

Dominant natural gas gatherer/processor (Transco pipeline -- largest US natural gas pipeline). US nat gas exports growing 30% by 2027. ~4.5% yield.

## 3. Tanker / Shipping -- The Biggest Immediate Winner

### The Thesis
This is the most directly impacted sector. EIA data (March 26, 2026) confirms VLCC rates from Middle East to Asia hit *multi-decade highs*. But critically, rates from the Americas (US Gulf Coast) also surged to record highs due to:
- Fewer available vessels (many trapped in the Persian Gulf)
- Longer routes as oil trade reroutes from Middle East to US Gulf/Atlantic Basin/West Africa
- Higher ton-miles = higher revenue per ship

### Top Pick: **International Seaways (INSW)**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Fleet | Modern VLCC and Suezmax fleet, primarily trading Atlantic Basin/US Gulf routes |
| Why INSW over FRO | Frontline (FRO) has heavier Middle East/Persian Gulf exposure; some FRO vessels may be trapped or unable to trade the strait. INSW's fleet is better positioned on the Atlantic/Americas routes that are seeing the highest demand surge |
| Earnings leverage | At current spot rates, INSW earns its entire market cap in ~1-2 years of cashflow |
| Shareholder returns | Aggressive buybacks + variable dividends. Management returns excess cash immediately |
| Dividend yield | Variable; likely 15-25%+ annualized at current rates |
| Valuation | Likely still trading below NAV (net asset value of fleet) despite rate surge |
| Risk | Rates are cyclical. If Hormuz reopens, rates normalize quickly. But even partial closure sustains elevated rates |

**Why not Frontline (FRO):** FRO is the largest pure-play tanker company. But its heavier exposure to Middle East routes means (a) more vessels at risk of being stranded, and (b) higher war-risk insurance costs eating into margins. INSW's Atlantic positioning is more advantageous right now.

### Runner-up: **Teekay Tankers (TNK)**

Mid-size tanker fleet (Aframax/LR2). These mid-size vessels are more flexible for rerouting through alternative routes. Strong buyback program.

## 4. Defense / Military -- Sustained Persian Gulf Presence

### The Thesis
"Operation Epic Fury" and the ongoing naval presence required to enforce the blockade/protect shipping means elevated defense spending. The Jones Act waiver by DHS on March 17 signals the military logistics chain is already strained. This is a multi-year spending cycle, not a one-quarter event.

### Top Pick: **Huntington Ingalls (HII)**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Moat | Only builder of US aircraft carriers and one of two nuclear submarine builders. Literally irreplaceable |
| Why now | A sustained Persian Gulf naval operation requires: ship maintenance, repair, replacement. HII's backlog grows with every carrier deployment |
| Backlog | Multi-year backlog already exceeding $45B+ |
| Dividend yield | ~2.5% |
| Valuation | Has historically traded at a discount to LMT/NOC/RTX because of slower growth; this crisis closes that gap |
| Unique angle | Unlike missiles (RTX) or aircraft (LMT), you can't quickly ramp ship production. HII's capacity constraint *is* its moat |

**Why not LMT/RTX:** Lockheed Martin and RTX will benefit broadly from defense spending. But HII is the most specific, least-substitutable beneficiary of a *naval* operation. Every extended carrier deployment accelerates maintenance cycles.

### Runner-up: **RTX Corporation (RTX)**

Broad defense/aerospace. Patriot missile systems deployed in the Gulf. Pratt & Whitney engines on F-35s. ~2.2% yield. More liquid, more diversified.

## 5. Refiners -- The Nuanced Winners

### The Thesis
Counter-intuitive: refiners *benefit* from Hormuz closure, at least in the short term. EIA confirms:
- Distillate crack spreads hit $1.42/gal in March (vs. $0.68/gal 5-year avg -- more than 2x)
- Refinery utilization exceeded the 5-year range in Q1 2026
- Brent-WTI spread widened to $25/b, meaning US refiners buying WTI-priced crude have a massive cost advantage over European/Asian refiners paying Brent

### Top Pick: **Valero (VLO)**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Why Valero | Largest independent US refiner. Pure refining play (no upstream exposure to confuse the picture) |
| Crude slate | Configured for medium/heavy sour crude -- exactly the grades being displaced from Middle East. Can source from Canada (TMX pipeline), Mexico, Venezuela as alternatives |
| Crack spread leverage | Every $1/bbl improvement in crack spreads adds ~$500M+ to annual EBITDA |
| Current margins | Distillate cracks at 2x historical average = massive windfall earnings in Q1/Q2 2026 |
| Export advantage | US exports of refined products to Europe surging (EIA confirmed increased distillate exports to Europe) |
| Dividend yield | ~3-3.5% |
| Buybacks | Aggressive share repurchaser; will deploy windfall cash |
| Risk | If crude prices spike so high that demand destruction kicks in, refining volumes fall. But current level ($118 Brent / ~$93 WTI) is still below demand-destruction thresholds |

**Why not MPC:** Marathon Petroleum (MPC) is excellent too, but has midstream exposure (MPLX) that muddies the pure refining thesis. Valero is the cleaner play on crack spread expansion.

## 6. Petrochemicals / Downstream -- Mixed Bag

### The Thesis
Higher feedstock costs hurt petrochemical margins *unless* the company has access to cheap US natural gas liquids (ethane, propane) as feedstock. US NGL-based crackers have a structural advantage over oil-based naphtha crackers in Europe/Asia.

### Top Pick: **Dow (DOW)** -- but with caveats

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Advantage | Largest US ethylene producer. US crackers use ethane (cheap, abundant from US nat gas) while global competitors use naphtha (expensive, tied to crude oil). This cost advantage *widens* as oil prices rise |
| Dividend yield | ~5-6% |
| Caveat | Dow's European operations use naphtha, offsetting some US advantage. Overall earnings impact is mixed |
| Better pure play | If you want pure US ethane cracker exposure, look at smaller names, but none are as liquid as DOW |

**Overall verdict:** Petrochems are **not** a high-conviction play. The winners are offset by losers within the same companies. If forced to pick, DOW benefits on net, but it's not a clean trade.

### Losers: **LyondellBasell (LYB)**
More European exposure, heavier naphtha cracker mix. Gets hurt by higher crude-linked feedstocks. Avoid.

## 7. Alternative / Renewable Energy -- Accelerated Transition

### The Thesis
$118/b Brent makes every alternative energy source more economically competitive. Gasoline at $3.99/gal pushes consumers toward EVs. Policymakers face pressure to accelerate energy independence. But the relationship is not instant -- this is a 12-24 month thesis, not a Q1 trade.

### Top Pick: **Cameco (CCJ)** -- Uranium/Nuclear

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Why nuclear | Nuclear is baseload, carbon-free, and the only alternative that can truly replace fossil fuels at scale for electricity. High energy prices + AI data center buildout = massive electricity demand growth |
| Cameco's position | World's largest pure-play uranium producer (mines in Canada/Kazakhstan). Controls ~18% of global production |
| Demand drivers | New reactor builds (China, India, Europe re-embracing nuclear), SMR development, life extensions of existing fleet |
| This crisis specifically | European nations that were already reconsidering nuclear (Germany, Belgium) now have another energy security argument. Japan is restarting reactors faster |
| Dividend yield | ~0.3% (growth stock, not income) |
| Risk | Uranium is volatile; spot vs. long-term contract pricing can diverge |

**Why not solar (ENPH/FSLR):** Solar companies face tariff uncertainty, Chinese competition, and are more dependent on residential construction cycles. Nuclear is the higher-conviction energy security play in a geopolitical crisis.

### Runner-up: **First Solar (FSLR)**

US-manufactured thin-film solar. Benefits from "energy independence" narrative and is insulated from Chinese panel tariffs. But valuation already rich.

| Metric | Value |

| Why non-obvious | Nobody talks about water when they discuss oil. But you can't drill without it. As US drilling accelerates, WTTR's volumes and pricing grow |
| Dividend yield | ~1.5% |

## Key Risk: How This Ends Matters

The entire thesis depends on the **duration** of the Hormuz closure:

- **Scenario A: Quick resolution (weeks)** -- Tankers and refiners give back gains. OFS/midstream thesis weakened. Defense unaffected (spending already authorized). *Probability: Low.* The assassination of Khamenei and regime-change objective suggests this is not a limited engagement.

- **Scenario B: Prolonged closure (months)** -- All picks above play out. US becomes swing producer/exporter. Tanker rates stay elevated. Defense budget escalates. *Probability: Medium-High.* This is the base case.

- **Scenario C: Escalation (wider war)** -- Oil above $150. Demand destruction kicks in. Recession risk rises. Defense wins big. Energy wins near-term but macro risk increases. *Probability: Medium.* Depends on Iran's remaining military capacity and proxy responses.

**Position sizing should reflect scenario uncertainty.** Tankers (INSW) and refiners (VLO) are the most time-sensitive trades. Midstream (ET) and defense (HII) are more durable across scenarios.
