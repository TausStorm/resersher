# AI-Generated Synthetic Pornography: The "Victimless" vs. Normalization Debate

## Research Summary

This document examines the ethical, psychological, legal, and philosophical dimensions of fully AI-generated pornography (not deepfakes of real people, but entirely synthetic/fictional content), with particular emphasis on the CSAM dimension.

---

## 1. The "Victimless" Argument

### Core Claims

The strongest arguments that synthetic AI pornography involving no real person is harmless:

**a) No direct victim exists.** Unlike real pornography involving exploitation, trafficking, or coercion, fully synthetic content is generated by algorithms. No person was harmed, coerced, trafficked, or exploited in its creation. This is the fundamental libertarian/free-speech argument.

**b) The "Safety Valve" / Cathartic Substitute Theory.** The most controversial pro-argument: that providing synthetic material to people with deviant urges could *prevent* real-world offenses by offering a substitute outlet.

- **Milton Diamond** (University of Hawaii) published research in *Archives of Sexual Behavior* (2010) showing that in the Czech Republic, Japan, and Denmark, legalization and availability of pornography (including simulated content) correlated with *decreased* rates of sex crimes, including child sex abuse. His research indicated that "the incidence of child sex abuse has fallen considerably since 1989, when child pornography became readily accessible" in the Czech Republic. Diamond explicitly suggested that "artificially produced materials might serve a purpose" as a substitute.
  - Source: Diamond, M.; Jozifkova, E.; Weiss, P. (2010). "Pornography and Sex Crimes in the Czech Republic." *Archives of Sexual Behavior*, 40(5), 1037-1043.

- A study by **Long, Alison & McManus** (2013) in *Sexual Abuse: A Journal of Research and Treatment* found that child pornography was used by some non-contact sex offenders as a "cathartic release" -- a substitute for physically offending. Non-contact offenders tended to collect large amounts of lower-level material, while contact offenders had smaller collections of more extreme material.
  - Source: Long et al. (2013). "Child Pornography and Likelihood of Contact Abuse." *Sexual Abuse*, 25(4), 370-395.

- A **Swiss study** by Endrass & Urbaniok (2009) reviewing 231 men charged with viewing child pornography found that those without prior sexual convictions were "unlikely to sexually assault a child." Only 1% committed a hands-on offense in the six years before or after the police operation. Author **Frank Urbaniok** stated: "Our results support the assumption that these consumers, in fact, form a distinct group of sex offenders. Probably, the motivation for consuming child pornography differs from the motivation to physically assault minors."
  - Source: Endrass, J.; Urbaniok, F. (2009). "The consumption of Internet child pornography and violent and sex offending." *BMC Psychiatry*, 9(43).

**c) Free speech / First Amendment protection.** In *Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition* (2002), the U.S. Supreme Court struck down the Child Pornography Prevention Act's ban on virtual child pornography that did not involve real children, ruling 6-3 that it violated the First Amendment. Justice Kennedy wrote that the CPPA "prohibits speech despite its serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value" and that virtual images "record no crime and create no victims by their production." The Court explicitly noted that *New York v. Ferber* (1982) banned child pornography because of harm to real children in its production, not because the content was inherently without value.
  - Source: *Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition*, 535 U.S. 234 (2002).

**d) The Canadian Civil Liberties Association argument:** Responding to Canada's committee finding that fictional pornography could be used for grooming, the CCLA argued that "pedophiles have been known to resort to candy as well. It is not possible or desirable to outlaw whatever might be used or abused in such a situation."

### Who Makes These Arguments

- Civil liberties organizations (ACLU, CCLA, Free Speech Coalition, EFF)
- Some academic sexologists (Milton Diamond, Frank Urbaniok)
- Free speech / First Amendment scholars
- Some forensic psychologists who study offender populations

## 2. The Normalization / Escalation Argument Against

### Key Research Findings

**a) Pornography Escalation Theory**

Research on pornography effects consistently finds (though with caveats about causation vs. correlation):

- A 2008 longitudinal study of 341 convicted child molesters found that "pornography's use correlated significantly with their rate of sexually re-offending. Frequency of pornography use was primarily a further risk factor for higher-risk offenders, and use of highly deviant pornography correlated with increased recidivism risk for all groups."
  - Source: Kingston, D.A. et al. (2008). "Pornography use and sexual aggression." *Aggressive Behavior*, 34(4), 1-11.

- Two 2016 neurology reviews found evidence of addiction-related brain changes in internet pornography users, described as "desensitization to reward," "dysfunctional anxiety response," and "impulsiveness."
  - Sources: Kraus et al. (2016); Kuhn & Gallinat (2016). Reviews in neuropsychopharmacology.

- A 2015 meta-analysis found that pornography was associated with sexual aggression globally, with violent pornography being the strongest driver.
  - Source: Wright et al. (2016). Meta-analysis on pornography and sexual aggression.

**b) The "Gateway" Hypothesis**

- The **National District Attorneys Association of America** states: "In light of the documented link between individuals who view child pornography and individuals who actually molest children, each child pornography case should be viewed as a red flag to the possibility of actual child molestation."

- **John Carr**, founding member of the UK Home Secretary's Internet Task Force on Child Protection, stated: "Many pedophiles acknowledge that exposure to child abuse images fuels their sexual fantasies and plays an important part in leading them to commit hands-on sexual offenses against children."
  - Source: Carr, J. (2004). *Child abuse, child pornography and the internet*. NCH Report.

- The **Mayo Clinic** reports that "30% to 80% of individuals who viewed child pornography and 76% of individuals who were arrested for Internet child pornography had molested a child," though they note the difficulty of establishing directionality.
  - Source: Hall, R.C.W. & Hall, R.C.W. (2007). "A Profile of Pedophilia." *Mayo Clinic Proceedings*, 82(4), 457-471.

- A study by the **Federal Bureau of Prisons** concluded that "many Internet child pornography offenders may be undetected child molesters." (Note: the study was controversially withdrawn by Bureau officials before publication over concerns about misinterpretation.)

**c) Desensitization Research**

- Sexual scripting theory (Simon & Gagnon, 1986) proposes that sexual behavior follows "scripts" acquired from media, including pornography. Repeated exposure to extreme content shifts what is perceived as normal.

- **Neil Malamuth** (UCLA) found that quantity of pornographic material viewed was positively correlated with the degree to which men endorsed sexual assault. Check (1984) found that more exposure to pornography led to higher acceptance of rape myths and violence against women.
  - Malamuth has argued that "porn is like alcohol: whether it's bad for you depends on who you are" -- increasing violence risk in a small subset of predisposed individuals.

- The ICD-11 added pornography to **Compulsive Sexual Behavior Disorder** (CSBD), recognizing problematic patterns of use, though this is classified as a compulsion, not an addiction.

**d) Fantasy Rehearsal and Behavior**

- The *confluence model of sexual aggression* suggests that pornography can influence sexually aggressive behavior particularly in men with "fragile masculinity" and those who are already sexually promiscuous.

- The *script acquisition, activation, application model* (3AM) proposes that behaviors depicted in pornographic content are acquired as "scripts" that are then mirrored in real life.

### Critical Caveats

- **Dennis Howitt** (Loughborough University, 1995) argues: "One cannot simply take evidence that offenders use and buy pornography as sufficient to implicate pornography causally in their offending. The most reasonable assessment based on the available research literature is that the relationship between pornography, fantasy and offending is unclear."

- **McKee, Litsou, Byron & Ingham** (2022) in a comprehensive book review note: "Much of the relevant research we identified on the relationship between consumption of pornography and aspects of healthy sexual development misinterpreted correlation as causality."

- **Ferguson & Hartley** (2009, updated 2020) concluded in a meta-analysis that "mainstream pornography could not be linked to sexual violence and was associated with reductions in sexual violence at the societal level." Small correlations between violent pornography and sexual aggression could not differentiate causation from selection effects.

- **Emily F. Rothman** (author of *Pornography and Public Health*, 2021) notes that "five studies found that the sexual violence perpetrators had seen less pornography than other criminals."

## 3. The CSAM Dimension

This is where the debate is most acute and most one-sided among experts and organizations.

### What the Data Shows

The **Internet Watch Foundation (IWF)** -- the UK's authority on online child sexual abuse imagery -- released their 2026 report with alarming findings:

- In 2025, **8,029 AI-generated images and videos** were assessed as showing realistic child sexual abuse
- AI-generated child sexual abuse **videos** increased by **26,385%** from 2024 to 2025 (from 13 to 3,443)
- **65% of AI videos** were classified as Category A (most extreme: penetrative sexual activity, sadism)
- Girls comprised **97%** of illegal AI-generated images
- AI CSAM chatbots are accessible on the clear web, encouraging simulated abuse scenarios
- Generative models are being trained on real abuse imagery, directly re-victimizing survivors
- The "Deepfake Defence" has emerged: offenders claiming genuine evidence of contact abuse was AI-generated

Source: IWF AI CSAM Report 2026, "Harm Without Limits"

### What Child Protection Organizations Say

**IWF:** "AI CSAM fuels sexual interest in children, normalises extreme violence, and increases the risk of contact offending."

**NCMEC (National Center for Missing & Exploited Children):** Ernest Allen, then-president and CEO, stated after *Ashcroft v. FSC* that the decision "will result in the proliferation of child pornography in America, unlike anything we have seen in more than 20 years."

### Legal Status of Synthetic CSAM

**United States:**
- *Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition* (2002): Virtual child pornography is protected speech under the First Amendment IF it is not obscene
- **PROTECT Act of 2003** (Congress's response to Ashcroft): Criminalizes computer-generated images that are "virtually indistinguishable from that of a minor engaging in sexually explicit conduct" AND any depictions of minors in sexual situations that meet the **Miller obscenity test**
- First conviction: **Dwight Whorley** (Virginia) -- convicted for anime cartoons of children in sexual situations. Upheld by the Fourth Circuit in 2008
- **Christopher Handley** -- convicted for possession of manga depicting minors in sexual acts. Pleaded guilty, sentenced to 6 months plus 5 years probation

**United Kingdom:**
- **Coroners and Justice Act 2009**: Made possession of prohibited images of a minor (including cartoon/virtual) illegal, regardless of whether they are realistic
- **Crime and Policing Bill 2025**: New criminal offence for making, adapting, possessing, or supplying a "CSA image-generator"

**Canada:**
- Supreme Court in *R v Butler* (1992) ruled that while a direct causal link from obscenity to harm "may be difficult, if not impossible, to establish," it was "reasonable to presume a causal link to changes in attitudes and beliefs"
- *R v Sharpe* (2001): Chief Justice McLachlin maintained that "Parliament was justified in concluding that visual works of the imagination would harm children"
- Fictional child pornography has been illegal since 1993, prosecuted with same severity as real CSAM
- First cartoon CSAM conviction: **Gordon Chin** (2005), 18 months imprisonment

**Australia:** Victoria state law makes it illegal to publish imagery depicting a person "who is, or appears to be, a minor engaging in sexual activity"

### Arguments For Criminalizing Synthetic CSAM

1. **Normalization risk.** Even without a direct victim, synthetic CSAM normalizes the sexualization of children and could lower psychological barriers to contact offending
2. **Training data problem.** AI models generating CSAM may have been trained on real abuse imagery, creating a direct chain to real-world victimization
3. **Detection interference.** AI CSAM makes it harder for law enforcement to identify and rescue real victims. The "deepfake defense" allows offenders to claim real evidence is AI-generated
4. **Grooming tool.** Synthetic CSAM can be used to groom children by showing them that "this is normal"
5. **Re-victimization.** When AI models are trained on or fine-tuned with real victims' images (via LoRA), survivors are directly harmed again
6. **Scale and accessibility.** AI tools allow generation of infinite violations with unprecedented ease, removing technical barriers

### Arguments Against Criminalizing Synthetic CSAM (Minority Position)

1. **No direct victim in production** (the Ashcroft argument)
2. **Possible substitution effect** (Diamond's research)
3. **Free speech concerns** -- ACLU and First Amendment scholars argue content should not be banned based on its potential to persuade people to commit crimes
4. **Enforcement difficulties** -- how do you regulate content created privately on local machines?

### Expert Consensus

The overwhelming expert consensus among child protection organizations, law enforcement, and most psychologists is that AI-generated CSAM should be treated as illegal, regardless of whether real children were involved in its creation. The primary reasons: normalization, grooming utility, interference with investigations, and the practical reality that much AI CSAM is fine-tuned on real abuse imagery.

## 4. Expert Opinions

### Named Experts and Their Positions

**Milton Diamond** (University of Hawaii, sexologist): Pro-substitution theory. His research suggests artificially produced materials could reduce contact offending. Published in *Archives of Sexual Behavior* (2010).

**Neil Malamuth** (UCLA, psychology): Nuanced. Pornography increases aggression risk in a predisposed subset, not the general population. "Porn is like alcohol: whether it's bad for you depends on who you are."

**Dennis Howitt** (Loughborough University, UK): Skeptical of causal claims. The relationship between pornography, fantasy, and offending "is unclear."

**Fred Berlin** (Johns Hopkins, National Institute for the Study, Prevention and Treatment of Sexual Trauma): Critical of suppressing research on the topic. Expressed disapproval when the Federal Bureau of Prisons withdrew a study finding high overlap between CP viewers and molesters.

**Michael Seto** (Canadian forensic psychologist, University of Toronto): Found that of 201 men charged with child pornography offenses, 24% had committed prior offenses of sexual contact, but only 4% went on to commit subsequent sexual offenses.

**Frank Urbaniok** (Swiss forensic psychiatrist): Consumers of CP "form a distinct group of sex offenders" with very low recidivism rates (1% for hands-on, 4% for hands-off).

**Emily F. Rothman** (Boston University, author of *Pornography and Public Health*): Notes that "five studies found that the sexual violence perpetrators had seen less pornography than other criminals." Critical of the "porn as public health crisis" framing, calling it a "political stunt."

**John Carr** (UK Home Secretary's Internet Task Force): Strong pro-normalization position. Many pedophiles acknowledge that abuse images fuel their fantasies and play a role in hands-on offending.

**IWF Analysts** (2026 Report): On the frontline of assessment. Report that AI CSAM is often deliberately made to look imperfect to emulate amateur photography, making it indistinguishable from real abuse imagery. "Analysts often only recognize material as synthetic because they are familiar with the specific victims depicted."

**Justice Anthony Kennedy** (U.S. Supreme Court, *Ashcroft* majority): Virtual child pornography "records no crime and creates no victims by its production." Banning it goes beyond what *Ferber* authorized.

**Chief Justice Beverley McLachlin** (Supreme Court of Canada, *R v Sharpe*): "Parliament was justified in concluding that visual works of the imagination would harm children."

## 5. Analogies and Precedents

### Violent Video Games

**Brown v. Entertainment Merchants Association** (2011): The U.S. Supreme Court struck down (7-2) a California law banning sales of violent video games to minors. Justice Scalia wrote that "video games qualify for First Amendment protection" and that the state failed to demonstrate a compelling link between violent video games and effects on children. The epidemiological evidence parallels the pornography debate -- decades of research have failed to establish clear causation between fictional violence and real-world violent behavior.

Key parallel: The state argued violent video games could cause real harm through normalization. The Court rejected this, finding the evidence insufficient. But the Court also specifically noted that violence, unlike obscenity, has never been treated as unprotected speech -- a distinction that matters for AI pornography.

### Written Content Depicting Illegal Acts

Fiction depicting murder, torture, child abuse, and other crimes has never been generally criminalized. Books like *Lolita* (Nabokov), *Tampa* (Nutting), and countless crime novels depict illegal acts in graphic detail. The distinction drawn by courts: the content itself is speech; only content that fails the Miller obscenity test or involves real victims in production can be restricted.

### The Ashcroft Decision's Reasoning

The *Ashcroft* Court made a critical distinction:
- **Ferber** (1982) banned child pornography because of the harm to real children in making it
- The CPPA extended this to content that involved no real children
- The Court rejected this extension, noting that the government "cannot constitutionally premise legislation on the desirability of controlling a person's private thoughts"

However, the **PROTECT Act of 2003** successfully closed part of this gap by requiring that virtual CP meet the Miller obscenity test -- which it almost certainly would, given that sexual content involving children has no "serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value" and would be found "patently offensive" by community standards.

### The Czech Republic "Natural Experiment"

Diamond's research on the Czech Republic is cited by both sides:
- Pro-substitution: Legalization of pornography correlated with *decreased* child sex abuse
- Anti-substitution: Correlation is not causation; many other social factors changed simultaneously (fall of communism, economic development, increased reporting mechanisms, etc.)

## 6. The Slippery Slope Concern

### Where Is the Line?

If we accept that AI-generated content depicting *illegal acts* with fictional entities is acceptable because "no real person was harmed":

- **AI-generated CSAM?** Most jurisdictions say NO, even without real children
- **AI-generated revenge porn of fictional people who resemble real people?** Gray area
- **AI-generated content depicting torture, murder, terrorism?** Currently legal in most jurisdictions
- **AI-generated content used for training or rehearsal of criminal acts?** Potentially prosecutable as conspiracy or planning

### Who Draws the Line?

The current framework across jurisdictions generally works as follows:

1. **Content involving real victims** -- illegal regardless of medium
2. **Obscene content (Miller test)** -- illegal regardless of whether victims are real (this captures most AI CSAM)
3. **Non-obscene fictional content depicting illegal acts** -- generally protected speech
4. **Content used as a tool for criminal acts** (grooming, planning) -- prosecutable based on use, not content

### The AI Escalation Problem

What makes AI different from previous debates about fictional content:

1. **Photorealism.** AI-generated content is now often indistinguishable from real photographs, unlike cartoons or written fiction
2. **Scale.** AI enables generation of effectively infinite content with zero marginal cost
3. **Personalization.** AI can generate content targeting specific individuals, scenarios, or fetishes
4. **Training data contamination.** AI models may incorporate real abuse imagery in their training
5. **Democratization.** Unlike film production, anyone with a computer can generate this content
6. **Detection difficulty.** Law enforcement cannot easily distinguish AI-generated from real content, creating the "deepfake defense"

The UK's response (Crime and Policing Bill 2025) may represent the emerging consensus: criminalize not just the content but the *tools* -- making it an offense to possess or supply a "CSA image-generator."
